last week I filled out a registration form for an Ivan Illich centenary gathering at a monastery in Tuscany. scholars, theologians, a few activists. standard fields — name, affiliation, country.

I wrote "network state."

half a joke. I sat with it for ten minutes before hitting submit. the honest answer — "I don't know anymore" — wouldn't fit in the field.

## the country field

I've rebuilt my identity across three countries. Belarus to Kyiv to Lisbon. each time, the thing that answered "what do you do?" and "where are you from?" got wiped clean.

I built a startup in Kyiv — users, a team, enough traction to have an answer that satisfied a room. when the war started in February 2022, I left behind the country that made all of it legible. in Lisbon I had a LinkedIn profile describing a person from a place that no longer functioned as a place.

I'm not romanticizing this. I got out with savings and a working passport. a lot of people got neither. but even with every advantage, when the place that validated your identity disappears, you find out fast how much of you was actually yours.

less than I thought.

the third reset is happening now. building something that doesn't need a country to validate it.

a friend is going through his own version. decade of backend experience. told me last week he feels like an intern. his junior colleague matches his output using Cursor. nobody moved countries. the skill that defined him just got repriced overnight.

same question, different trigger. when the thing that labeled you disappears, what's underneath?

## the authorship gap

earlier this year I co-authored [the context gap](https://gap.aimindset.org) — ten loops where machine capability and human experience pull apart. loop 7 is the one that won't leave me alone: authorship.

I tracked my own screen for sixteen days last month. every prompt, every tab switch. day three I caught myself asking Claude questions I already knew the answers to. day nine I'd stopped forming my own take before checking. handing off the thirty-second problems. each time, harder to trust that I had a take worth having.

I still do this. haven't fixed it. I vibe-code, I ship with it, I'm in it. I see it happen and choose convenience anyway, most days.

Stack Overflow's traffic collapsed 35% in a year. the infrastructure that let people teach themselves to code is thinning right when more people need it. fewer people, more output, and the thing that used to connect craft to community — disappearing in the middle.

that's the gap. when labor gets cheaper, identity gets more expensive.

## the decision stack

I DJ. played a set at a Belarusian spring-calling ritual in Lisbon last month — didn't compose a single track. the set was mine: selection, sequencing, reading the room. nobody asks a DJ "did you write this?" they ask if it landed.

code is going the same way. fast.

using AI well is its own skill — the debugging, the prompting, the knowing when to override. I'm not dismissing that. but skill and identity are different questions.

your output is a commodity. your decision stack is yours. what problem you chose. which features you killed. which constraints you accepted. which risks you own. the distance between "I made an app" and "I can tell you exactly why this app exists and what I'd change."

the model doesn't lose sleep when your product fails. you do. that asymmetry is authorship.

I wrote about the adjacent problem in [a dead priest and your AI problem](https://embassy.svit.la/p/a-dead-priest-and-your-ai-problem) — can you still do the thing without the tool? but capability and identity are different problems. you can pass that test and still feel the ground shift as the market reprices your craft.

I [went from seven figures to insolvent](https://embassy.svit.la/p/fork-yourself-before-the-state) because I had output and no decisions underneath it. shipped everything, chose nothing. my identity was "the guy who ships." when shipping stopped working, there was nothing there. no sovereignty over my attention. just output and a story I told about it.

## what survives

Illich had a name for the space where you do things your own way, outside institutional permission: the vernacular domain. the part of life you author because you chose to, because you maintain it, because you can explain it. the monastery gathering is about his centenary. a hundred years later, the question is the same — can you name what's yours when the institutions stop naming it for you?

the [one person state](https://embassy.svit.la/p/the-ritual-kaleidoscope) I've been building in this newsletter grows from that idea and from my own wreckage. identity as infrastructure — rituals, decisions, config. something that holds whether the market prices your labor at $200/hour or $20.

for people who've rebuilt themselves across countries and languages, this isn't new. authoring yourself from fragments is what diaspora builders already practice. the decision stack is just a name for what they've been doing.

## what this looks like on a tuesday

I journal my decisions. thirty seconds after each one. why this architecture. why I killed that feature. why I'm writing about a dead priest instead of making a LinkedIn carousel about AI productivity.

last week: killed the recommendation engine in the MVP. it needed user data I don't have and would train users to expect personalization I can't sustain. less engagement, cleaner onboarding. thirty seconds of writing. the one artifact from that build week no model can claim.

nobody pays you for this. it doesn't scale. it won't trend.

but it's the only practice that answered the question on the monastery form.

country: the decisions I've made and the corners I've chosen to stand in.
