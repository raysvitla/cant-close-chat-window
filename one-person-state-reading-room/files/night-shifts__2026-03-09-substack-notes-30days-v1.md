## DAY 8 — March 16, Sunday

you signed a Terms of Service you never read when you were born. it's called the social contract.

it dictates where you can work. how much of your income you keep. what you can put in your body. who you can marry in some places.

the question 404Embassy keeps circling: how do you refactor a system you can't take offline?

## DAY 17 — March 25, Tuesday
**Source:** adapted from LinkedIn Day 22

**NOTE:**
when you hate how things work, you have two options. leave, or stay and fight to change it.

Albert Hirschman called these "exit" and "voice" in 1970.

I'm an exit person. always have been. bad job? quit. bad city? move. bad system? build a new one. 404Embassy is the most "exit" project possible.

but 17 days of writing in public taught me something: you can't build an alternative without voice. without explaining, persuading, arguing, listening. pure exit is just running away with a business plan.

if you haven't read "Exit, Voice, and Loyalty" — it's 55 years old and still the best framework for understanding why people leave organizations or countries.

are you more exit or voice?

## DAY 19 — March 27, Thursday
**Source:** adapted from LinkedIn Day 25

**NOTE:**
Ivan Illich argued in 1973 that tools should serve people, not the other way around. he called them "convivial tools" — tools that expand what you can do without creating dependence.

a bicycle is convivial. it makes you faster without making you dependent.

now look at AI through this lens. ChatGPT makes you faster. but it requires internet, a subscription, a company's server, that company's policy decisions, and trust that they won't change terms tomorrow.

the convivial version of AI would run on your machine. owned by you. no subscription. no terms of service.

we're building the car version of AI and calling it liberation. Illich would have had opinions.

"Tools for Conviviality" — 1973, free PDF online. reads like it was written about the AI debate.

## DAY 21 — March 29, Saturday
**Source:** adapted from Threads Day 15

**NOTE:**
hot take: your HOA is a government.

it collects taxes (fees). it makes laws (bylaws). it enforces them (fines). it has elections that 4 people show up to. it controls your property.

the difference between an HOA and a country is scale and weapons.

this isn't a joke — it's actually the most honest way to understand how governance works. small groups making rules that affect your daily life, mostly run by whoever bothers to show up.

this is also why I think governance innovation will come from small communities, not nation-states. the HOA scale is where you can actually experiment. the nation-state scale is where experiments go to die in committee.
