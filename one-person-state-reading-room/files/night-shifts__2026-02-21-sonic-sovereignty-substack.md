# Sonic Sovereignty: Why Every Network State Needs a Sound — and How Belarus Could Build One First

Every sovereign community in history has created its own sonic identity — from anthems to genres. Network states haven't done this yet. They build constitutions, tokenomics, visual brands — but forget about sound. This is a strategic mistake. And the Belarusian diaspora is uniquely prepared to become the first to do it consciously.

---

## Sovereignty Has a Sound

Every political community claiming self-determination goes through a stage of sonic crystallization — a moment when disparate people begin to sound as one.

**Israel** before statehood: kibbutz songs and "Hatikvah" as an audio anchor for the national project long before territory.

**Estonia** — the Singing Revolution (1988): 300,000 people sang folk songs. Literally — singing as a sovereign act. This is not a metaphor: song *replaced* an army.

**Afrobeats** as the sonic identity of the Nigerian diaspora: not just a genre, but economic infrastructure. London, Toronto, Atlanta became hubs through which the diaspora transmits cultural and economic power back home.

The pattern is clear: sonic identity always *precedes* political recognition. First people start sounding together, then they demand sovereignty.

## Sonic Sovereignty as a Concept

Researcher Kiera Ladner (and later Dylan Robinson) coined the term "sonic sovereignty" to describe how Indigenous peoples of Canada use hip-hop and music as a tool of self-determination.

The key insight: sonic sovereignty is an "embodied practice of Indigenous self-determination through musical expression."

This isn't about anthems or official soundtracks. It goes deeper:

- Sound as the right to self-determination — *how we sound*
- Sound as collective memory — *what we remember*
- Sound as territory — *where we exist, even without land*

For network states without territory, the last point is critical. **Sound can be territory when there is no land.**

## Belarus 2020: Unconscious Sonic Sovereignty

The Belarusian revolution of 2020 is already an example of sonic sovereignty. Nobody just called it that.

**Soviet rock legacy.** Viktor Tsoi's "Peremen!" became an anthem after two DJs — Kirill Galanov and Vladislav Sokolovsky — were forced to play at a pro-government event and blasted Tsoi instead. The sound lasted one minute before being cut off, but recordings on hundreds of phones became a meme. VIA Yabatskasatan — a parody name referencing Soviet vocal-instrumental ensembles — their song "Nursed on the Same Tit" riffed on Nautilus Pompilius's "Chained Together."

**Belarusian folk tradition.** Dai Darohu!'s "Kartokha" opens with a motif from Pesnyary's "Yasya Mowed Clover." A direct line from Soviet-Belarusian VIA to protest music.

**Electronic scene.** Ezhevika label — Minsk, 15 years, 163 releases — chill & indie electronic, annual "Belarus Electronic" compilations. The Spotify genre "belarusian electronic" shows 87 tracks in 2024, active growth since 2020.

These layers emerged organically, not by design. But they demonstrate readiness — the Belarusian diaspora already has sonic DNA.

## The Sonic Stack: A Framework

By analogy with a tech stack, I propose a Sonic Stack — a set of sonic identity layers for a network state:

**Layer 5: Generative.** AI models trained on community voices. Collective voice.

**Layer 4: Ritual.** Sounds of events, ceremonies, gatherings. "What does our assembly sound like?"

**Layer 3: Diaspora.** Fusion genres born at the intersection of cultures. New sound from collision with host culture.

**Layer 2: Memory.** Archive of homeland sounds. Field recordings, folk songs, voices.

**Layer 1: Resistance.** Protest music. "Peremen!" The first collective sound.

Most diasporas get stuck at Layers 1-2. Afrobeats reached Layers 3-4. Nobody has reached Layer 5 yet.

## AI as a Tool for Collective Voice

Two key precedents:

**Holly Herndon — Holly+.** Trained an AI model on her community's voices, then created a DAO to govern AI voice usage. "It takes an entire community to raise this AI, whose entire worldview is really based on the training material we provide." Caroline Polachek called it waking up the art and music community to "this window of time we have."

**Grimes — Elf.Tech.** Open-sourced her own voice. 15,000+ voice transformations in the first weeks. 50/50 royalty split on AI-generated tracks.

Both experiments involve one artist sharing their voice. But what if the voice belonged not to an artist, but to a community? What if 500 Belarusians from the diaspora trained an AI model on their voices — singing, speech, field recordings — and created the **collective voice of the Belarus Network State?**

## A Concrete Proposal: Belarus Sonic Stack

**Phase 1: Memory Layer (0-6 months).** Create an open-source sound archive — Belarusian trasianka, folk songs, 2020 protest recordings, sounds of Minsk and villages. Bandcamp/IPFS collection, curated through 404Embassy.

**Phase 2: Diaspora Layer (6-12 months).** Belarusian producers from different diaspora cities — Warsaw, Vilnius, Lisbon, Tbilisi — create tracks, fusion genres. GIRA SOL (Ray & Oleksa in Lisbon) as proof of concept. A compilation: "Belarus Network Sound Vol.1," each track from a different diaspora city.

**Phase 3: Generative Layer (12-18 months).** Train an open-source voice model on voluntary recordings from Belarus Network State participants. Each participant records 10 minutes of their voice. The model trains on the collective sound. Any participant can use the "voice of the nation" in their tracks. DAO governance, Holly+ style. Each voice contributor gets voting power.

## Why Belarus First

There is no territory — which means a new type of territory is needed. Sound is the ideal candidate. The electronic scene already exists (Ezhevika, 15 years). DJ culture thrives across the diaspora, from Lisbon to Warsaw. And Belarus Network State 2030 provides the strategic framework where a sonic layer fits naturally.

Estonian Singing Revolution (1988) + Holly Herndon's Holly+ (2021) + Balaji's Network State (2022) = the formula for sonic sovereignty of a digital nation.
