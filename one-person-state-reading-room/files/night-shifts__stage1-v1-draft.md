# STAGE 1: V1 DRAFT (~1100 words)

---

Last autumn I filled out a registration form for an Ivan Illich centenary gathering at a monastery in Tuscany. Scholars, theologians, a few activists. Standard fields — name, affiliation, country.

I wrote "network state."

Half a joke. But I sat with it for ten minutes before I hit submit. Because the honest answer — "I don't know anymore" — wouldn't fit.

## three resets

I've rebuilt my identity from scratch three times.

First: Minsk to Kyiv. I left Belarus in my twenties after getting too close to how the dictatorship actually works. In Kyiv I built a dating app called WAKA — 100K users, $300K raised, a team, a product, a reason to answer "what do you do?" at parties. Identity: founder.

Second: Kyiv to Lisbon. February 2022. War. Forced migration. I left behind the country that had validated everything I'd built. The users, the network, the language I thought in when I was solving problems. In Lisbon I was nobody with a LinkedIn profile that described a person from a place that no longer functioned.

Third reset is happening now. I'm building something that doesn't depend on a country validating it. A one person state — identity as infrastructure you carry. Not "where are you from" but "what do you decide and why."

When the country that validated your identity disappears, you find out how much of you was yours.

## the decision that no model makes

Last year I wrote an article about a dead priest — Ivan Illich, conviviality tools, what happens when AI becomes a radical monopoly. I used Claude to help write it. The output was AI-assisted. The decision — write about a dead Catholic priest for Web3 founders — that's the part no language model would generate.

Sajay Samuel, the custodian of Illich's intellectual legacy, cold-emailed me because of that piece. Not because I'm a marketer. Not because I code. Because I thought at the intersection where nobody else was standing.

That's the thing about identity in 2026. Your output is a commodity. Labor is cheap and getting cheaper — 92% of US developers use AI coding tools now. The thing that's yours is the decision layer. What you choose to build. What you refuse. What weird intersection you stand at that makes people who've spent forty years in a completely different field write you an email.

## the bridge

I was a John Smith Trust Fellow a few years back. The program gathers people from post-Soviet countries — mostly politicians, NGO founders, social project leaders. I was almost the only one there from the startup world.

Turned out that was the point. I could talk to all of them. The politician running rural education reform, the activist building legal aid networks, the social entrepreneur trying to make cooperatives work in Georgia. I understood their constraints because I'd shipped products under similar ones — bad infrastructure, no capital, governments that actively work against you.

They weren't drawn to my marketing skills. They were drawn to the bridge. Someone who could look at a systemic problem and see both the policy angle and the deployment angle.

My identity isn't "marketer" or "coder" or "founder." It's "the person who sees the bridge." Three country resets taught me that. You don't discover your actual identity until everything that labeled you — your job title, your country, your network — gets stripped away.

## identity is the new expensive thing

I wrote about this pattern in the ritual kaleidoscope — when time inflates, when vibe coders ship without roots, the thing that holds value is the practice underneath. The rituals. The config. The decision stack.

Your decision stack: what problem you chose, which features you killed, which constraints you accepted, which risks you own. I went from seven figures to insolvent because I had output without a stack underneath it. Shipped everything. Decided nothing.

The monastery form asked for my country. But the real question on every form now is: what do you stand on when the label disappears?

A friend with a decade of backend experience told me last week he feels like an intern. His junior colleague matches his output with Cursor. He's asking the country question without knowing it — when the skill that defined you gets commodified, what's left?

What's left is the same thing that was left when I landed in Lisbon with a suitcase and a Telegram contact list. Decisions. Taste. The weird intersections you've accumulated that no one else has.

## what I actually do

I journal my decisions. Thirty seconds after each one. Why this architecture. Why I killed that feature. Why I'm writing about a dead priest instead of doing a LinkedIn carousel about AI productivity.

Last week I registered for the Illich gathering. Country: network state. The organizers didn't flag it. Maybe they've seen weirder. Maybe it made perfect sense for a newsletter about identity infrastructure written by a Belarusian in Lisbon who got cold-emailed by a dead priest's intellectual heir.

The most expensive thing you own isn't your skills. It's not your output. It's the thing that survives when the country, the job title, and the tools all change at once.

Build that.
