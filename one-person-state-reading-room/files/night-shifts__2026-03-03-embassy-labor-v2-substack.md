Last autumn I filled out a registration form for an Ivan Illich centenary gathering at a monastery in Tuscany. Scholars, theologians, a few activists. Standard fields — name, affiliation, country.

I wrote "network state."

Half a joke. I sat with it for ten minutes before I hit submit. The honest answer — "I don't know anymore" — wouldn't fit in the field.

## three resets

I've rebuilt my identity from scratch three times.

Minsk to Kyiv. Left Belarus in my twenties after getting too close to how dictatorship works from the inside. Built a dating app called WAKA in Kyiv — 100K users, $300K raised, a team. For the first time I had an answer to "what do you do?" that satisfied a room. Identity: founder.

Kyiv to Lisbon. February 2022. War started on a Thursday. I left behind the country that validated everything I'd built. The users, the network, the language I dreamed in when I was debugging. In Lisbon I had a LinkedIn profile describing a person from a place that no longer functioned as a place.

I'm not romanticizing this. I got to Lisbon. I had savings and a passport that worked. A lot of people from Kyiv got neither. But even with every advantage, when the country that validated your identity disappears, you find out fast how much of you was actually yours. The answer was: less than I thought.

Third reset is now. Not rebuilding in another country. Building something that doesn't need a country to validate it.

## the cold email

I wrote [an article about Ivan Illich](https://embassy.svit.la/p/a-dead-priest-and-your-ai-problem) last year — conviviality, radical monopoly, what happens when AI tools become the only way to do the thing they replaced. I used Claude to write it. The output was AI-assisted.

The decision — write about a dead Catholic priest for people who build Web3 products — was mine. No model would generate that prompt.

Sajay Samuel, custodian of Illich's intellectual legacy, cold-emailed me because of that piece. Forty years of scholarship on conviviality, and he's writing to a Belarusian newsletter guy in Lisbon. Not because I market well. Because I stood at an intersection nobody else was standing at.

92% of US developers use AI coding tools now. Output is cheap. The decision layer — what you choose to build, what you refuse, what weird corner you stand in — is what's scarce.

## the bridge

I was a John Smith Trust Fellow. The program puts post-Soviet startup people, politicians, and NGO founders in the same room. I was nearly the only one from the tech world.

I could talk to all of them. The politician doing rural education reform, the activist building legal aid in Georgia. I'd shipped products under the same constraints they worked in — bad infrastructure, no capital, hostile governments. Three country resets stripped every professional label I had. What was left was a way of seeing problems: the bridge between the policy angle and the deployment angle. You don't find your actual operating system until the labels come off.

## identity after the price drop

A friend — decade of backend experience — told me last week he feels like an intern. His junior colleague matches his output using Cursor. He's going through his own reset, except nobody moved countries. The skill that defined him just got repriced overnight.

Same question, different trigger. When the thing that labeled you disappears, what's underneath?

I [went from seven figures to insolvent](https://embassy.svit.la/p/fork-yourself-before-the-state) because I had output and no decisions underneath it. Shipped everything, chose nothing. The [one person state](https://embassy.svit.la/p/the-ritual-kaleidoscope) I've been building in this newsletter grows from that wreckage. Illich called the version he cared about the vernacular domain — the space where you do things for yourself, in your own way, outside institutional permission. That's what the third reset is actually about. Not moving to another country. Learning to stand on something a country can't revoke.

## what this looks like on a tuesday

I journal my decisions. Thirty seconds after each one. Why this architecture. Why I killed that feature. Why I'm writing about a dead priest instead of making a LinkedIn carousel about AI productivity.

Last week: killed the recommendation engine in the MVP. It needed user data I don't have and would train users to expect personalization I can't sustain. Less engagement, cleaner onboarding. Thirty seconds of writing. The one artifact from that build week no model can claim.

Nobody pays you for this. It doesn't scale. It won't trend.

But it's the only practice that answers the question on the monastery form. Country: the decisions I've made and the intersections I've chosen to stand at. Portable not because it's light. Because it's mine.
