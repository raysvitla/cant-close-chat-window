# Diaspora Memoirist — Evaluation of "One Person State"

*Reading Ray Svitla's material as displacement literature*

---

## 1. Where is the emotional core?

The book Ray thinks he's writing is about sovereignty infrastructure. The book he's *actually* writing is about **the terror of disappearing — and the invention of a self that can survive disappearance.**

Underneath every chapter concept — phantom states, steganography of nations, body as territory — is a single, repeating scream: *I was erased, and I rebuilt myself, and then I was erased again, and I rebuilt myself again, and I'm afraid that if I stop building I will finally vanish.*

The name change tells you everything. Dmitry Levkovets became Ray Svitla. This is not a metaphor for sovereignty. This is the act itself — the moment a displaced person realizes their birth name belongs to a country that no longer belongs to them. "Ray" = beam. "Svitla" = light. He named himself after something that cannot be detained at a border. Something that has no passport. Something that exists only in motion.

The real emotional core is not sovereignty. It is **grief performed as engineering.** Every system Ray builds — the 8 life blocks, the year-as-system review, the "dispatcher vs DJ" framework — is a displaced person's attempt to create *internal territory* because all external territory has been taken from them. The frameworks aren't intellectual exercises. They're survival architecture. They're the emotional equivalent of a refugee sewing jewelry into a coat lining.

The sentence "I'm not a person, I'm a pipeline (and I'm learning to be human again)" — this is the most honest thing in all 300,000 words. This is the book. A man who turned himself into infrastructure because being a person in the places he lived was too dangerous, too painful, or too impermanent. And now, in Lisbon, in a marriage, in a DJ booth playing Belarusian spring songs for people who will never see Belarus again — he's trying to find his way back to being a body, not a system.

---

## 2. The memoir spine

These are not decorations. These are load-bearing walls:

**Wall 1: The name change.** This is your *In the Beginning*. Not as anecdote — as the central act of the book. The day Dmitry died and Ray was born. What was it like to sign a document erasing yourself? What did your mother say? What name do your Belarusian friends still use when they're drunk or angry or tender? A displaced person's name is their first border — the one they carry inside. Herta Müller wrote entire books about less.

**Wall 2: The night you left Kyiv.** Not the geopolitical context. Not the analysis of Russian aggression. The *night*. The bag. What you took, what you left. The sound of the door closing. Every displacement memoir lives or dies on its exodus scene. Alexievich would spend forty pages on this single night if she had it.

**Wall 3: WAKA's death.** "Shipped everything, chose nothing." This is not a startup failure story. This is a sovereignty failure story — the first time Ray built something that was supposed to be *his* territory (a company, a product, a community of 100,000 users) and watched it dissolve. The insolvency of WAKA is a rehearsal for the insolvency of nations. The emotional logic: *If I can't even keep a dating app alive, how do I keep a self alive?*

**Wall 4: Гуканне вясны in Lisbon.** A Belarusian spring-calling ritual performed in a country where spring doesn't need calling. This is the most potent image in all the material. It is the displaced person's essential act: performing the rituals of a homeland that exists now only in the performance. This is where "sonic sovereignty" stops being a concept and becomes something you can hear — a room full of exiles singing spring into existence because the real spring, the one in Minsk, has been stolen from them.

**Wall 5: The father-in-law's ketamine therapy.** War trauma treated with a dissociative drug. A man who survived the thing Ray fled from, now being healed by chemically leaving his body. This is the book's shadow self — the version of displacement that is not intellectual or entrepreneurial but physiological, irreversible, medical. Ray builds sovereignty frameworks. His father-in-law needs a doctor to help him leave the room his mind is trapped in. These two men at the same dinner table *is* the book.

**Wall 6: The 2020 protests — the last time Ray believed in collective sovereignty.** Before "one person state" there was "we, the people." The Tsepkalo campaign, the crowdfunded coat of arms — these are the acts of someone who still believed that sovereignty could be shared, that a nation could be saved by its citizens together. The book's intellectual framework (the node over the network) is built on the ruins of that belief. The reader needs to feel the collapse of "we" before "I" becomes necessary.

## 3. What's missing?

Here is what Alexievich would ask Ray, recorder running, sitting across from him in some café in Lisbon, patient and merciless:

**"Tell me about the language."** You organize Belarusian language celebrations. But what language do you dream in? What language do you fight with your wife in? What language comes out when you stub your toe, when you orgasm, when you're afraid? Displacement lives in the mouth. Every exile carries at least two languages, and the war between them is a war for the soul. Ray's material is full of *concepts* about language (vernacular domain, Illich's linguistic philosophy) but silent about his own. Does he speak Belarusian daily or only at events? Does Russian feel like the colonizer's tongue or like home? What happens to Belarusian in his mouth when he's been speaking Portuguese all day?

**"Tell me about the body."** Chapter 10 is called "body as first territory" but where is the body? Where is the physical experience of displacement — the way a new city smells wrong, the way your gut doesn't work for the first three months in a new country, the way your shoulders hold tension from years of not knowing if you're safe? Müller wrote about hunger as the body's knowledge of political violence. Ray's body has lived through three country resets. What does it know?

**"Tell me about what you lost."** Not abstractly. Specifically. The apartment in Minsk — what was on the walls? The friends who stayed — do you still talk? The ones who went to prison after 2020 — do you feel guilty that you left? The food your mother makes — can you find the ingredients in Lisbon? Displacement literature without inventory is philosophy, not memoir.

**"Tell me about your marriage."** You married during displacement. What does it mean to build a home with another person when you don't trust homes? What language do you say "I love you" in? Does your wife understand what it costs you to stay in one place? Two years married — that's a blink. What are you afraid of?

**"Tell me about the people who didn't leave."** The ones still in Minsk. The cousins, the school friends, the neighbors. What do they say when you call? Do they resent you? Do you resent them for staying? The diaspora memoir is always a triangle: the person who left, the place they left, and the people who remain. Right now the book has only two sides.

**"Tell me what sovereignty actually feels like at 3 AM."** Not the framework. The feeling. When the concept breaks down. When you're awake and jet-lagged in another country and you realize you don't know where your medical records are, you can't remember your old phone number, and no country on earth would send a search party if you vanished.

## 4. Displacement archetypes

Ray's story does not fit cleanly into the canonical categories:

- **Exile literature** (Brodsky, Nabokov, Kundera) — the expelled intellectual looking back at the lost homeland with literary precision. Ray is too young, too digital, too forward-looking. He's not mourning a lost literary culture. He's building apps.

- **Refugee literature** (Viet Thanh Nguyen, Mohsin Hamid) — the forcibly displaced person navigating the violence of arrival. Ray wasn't bombed out of Kyiv in the classic refugee sense, though the line between "chose to leave" and "had to leave" is exactly the line this book should interrogate.

- **Nomad literature** (Chatwin, Kapuściński) — the chosen wanderer. Too romantic. Ray didn't choose this. Or did he? This ambiguity is productive.

What Ray is actually writing is something I'd call **"post-state exile literature"** — a new category for people who were displaced not once but serially, who no longer believe in the nation as a container for identity, and who have turned inward not out of narcissism but because every external structure has failed them. The closest archetype is **the internal emigrant** — a concept from Soviet-era literature for people who left without leaving, who built private worlds inside hostile systems. Except Ray literally left, multiple times, and is now building the private world *as the explicit project.*

This is also **builder's exile** — distinct from the poet's exile or the soldier's exile. Ray processes displacement through systems, frameworks, startups, events. He builds his way out of grief. The book should name this archetype and own it, because there are millions of people doing exactly this — the Ukrainian developer in Warsaw, the Belarusian designer in Tbilisi, the Russian antiwar activist in Yerevan — all building, building, building, because building is how they keep from drowning.

## 5. Comparable books

**1. Viet Thanh Nguyen — *The Sympathizer* (2015)**
Displacement processed through split identity — a man who is always two things at once. What worked: the voice, the fury, the refusal to be grateful. What applies to Ray: the double consciousness of the exile who is never fully anywhere. The sovereignty framework is Ray's version of the Sympathizer's dual allegiance — a way of being loyal only to a self you invented.

**2. Dubravka Ugrešić — *The Culture of Lies* (1998) / *The Ministry of Pain* (2004)**
A Yugoslav exile who watched her country dissolve and refused to pick a side. What worked: the essayistic form that moves between memoir, criticism, and fury. What applies to Ray: Ugrešić understood that when your country disappears, genre disappears too. You can't write a straightforward memoir about a place that no longer exists. The hybrid form Ray is gravitating toward (personal + philosophical + manual) has precedent here.

**3. Svetlana Alexievich — *Secondhand Time* (2013)**
The Soviet collapse told through voices. What worked: the radical subjectivity — no framework, only testimony. What this warns Ray about: his book is the *inverse* of Alexievich. She let the voices speak without system. Ray is building system without enough voice. The book needs more Alexievich and less architecture. Let the people talk — the father-in-law, the uncle with the Spanish visa, the wife, the DJ partner, the Belarusians at Гуканне вясны.

**4. Kapka Kassabova — *Border* (2017)**
A Bulgarian-Scottish writer returns to the borders of her childhood — Bulgaria, Greece, Turkey. Displacement becomes geography becomes meditation. What worked: the braided structure of personal journey, deep history, and the voices of people who live on borders. This is the closest structural model for what Ray could do — each chapter rooted in a place, a person, and a concept, braided together.

**5. Valeria Luiselli — *Tell Me How It Ends: An Essay in 40 Questions* (2017)**
A writer translating for child migrants at the US border. Personal positioning + systemic analysis + burning moral urgency. What worked: the short, sharp form. 40 questions as structure. What applies to Ray: the book doesn't need to be 300 pages. The urgency of displacement resists bloat. Luiselli proved you could write a paradigm-shifting displacement book in under 100 pages.

## 6. Structural recommendation

**The current 11-chapter structure is intellectual scaffolding with no emotional foundation.** "The node," "the decision layer," "context gap" — these are concepts looking for a body to live in. The structure needs to be inverted.

**Recommended structure: The Braided Spiral.**

Each chapter begins with a *scene* — a specific moment in a specific place with specific sensory detail. The scene generates the question. The question opens into the framework. The framework is tested against another scene. The chapter closes with a return to the body — where Ray is now, what he's holding.

Think of it as three threads braided:

- **Thread 1: The body** (scenes, sensory memory, the physical facts of displacement)
- **Thread 2: The mind** (Illich, sovereignty theory, systems thinking)
- **Thread 3: The practice** (what Ray actually does — the events, the DJ sets, the name change, the life-system reviews)

Each chapter weaves all three. No chapter is purely intellectual. No chapter is purely memoir. The braiding is the point — because that's how displacement actually works. You don't think about sovereignty in the abstract. You think about it while standing in line at the SEF office in Lisbon holding a document that spells your dead name.

**Proposed chapter rhythm (not titles — emotional beats):**

1. **The door closing** — leaving (which time? all of them, overlaid). Establishes the serial displacement. Introduces the question: what survives?
2. **The name** — Dmitry becomes Ray. The first sovereign act. Illich enters here — vernacular identity vs. institutional identity.
3. **The thing I built** — WAKA. The startup as attempted homeland. Its failure as the failure of external sovereignty.
4. **The square** — 2020 protests. The last time "we" felt possible. The crowdfunded coat of arms as collective sovereignty's last stand.
5. **The invasion (someone else's and mine)** — Kyiv, February 2022. The father-in-law's war. The body's knowledge of danger. Body as territory begins here, in the body that fled.
6. **The frequency** — Sonic sovereignty. Гуканне вясны. The DJ booth as stateless territory. How displaced people build nations from sound.
7. **The gap** — Context gap. What is lost in every translation, every border crossing, every new language. The uncle's Spanish visa. The bureaucratic violence of displacement.
8. **The dead priest** — Illich. But told as a story of a man who also left — who went from Vienna to Cuernavaca to build something the institutions couldn't kill. Illich as fellow exile.
9. **The system** — The year-as-system review. The 8 blocks. "Dispatcher vs DJ." The confession: "I'm not a person, I'm a pipeline." The moment the sovereignty framework threatens to eat the person.
10. **The kitchen table** — The father-in-law on ketamine. The wife. The marriage as the smallest viable state. What sovereignty means when another person is inside the walls.
11. **Spring** — Гуканне вясны again, but now earned. The monastery form as a way of living, not a concept. The book ends in practice, not theory. You are the one person state. Now go live.

## 8. Warning

**The biggest trap: intellectualizing the wound.**

Every displaced writer faces this temptation. The pain of exile is so large, so formless, so unspeakable, that the mind reaches for frameworks the way a drowning person reaches for anything solid. Sovereignty theory, Illich, systems thinking — these are all ways of *not feeling* the displacement while appearing to address it.

Ray has 300,000 words of night research sessions. That is not writing. That is insomnia with footnotes. That is a displaced person staying up all night building systems because the alternative — lying in the dark in a country that isn't yours, with a name that isn't yours, missing a city that might not exist anymore — is unbearable.

The book must *go through* the pain, not around it. Every paragraph of theory must earn its place by answering a question the body asked first. If the reader can remove a concept and the book still stands, the concept is decoration. If the reader can remove a *scene* and the book collapses, the scene is structure.

Herta Müller wrote about totalitarianism through a handkerchief, a bread crumb, the angle of light in a labor camp. She didn't need frameworks. She had the thing itself. Ray has the thing itself — three exoduses, a murdered democracy, a name burned and rebuilt, a father-in-law whose mind was broken by a war — but he keeps reaching for the framework instead of the thing.

The secondary trap, closely related: **performing resilience instead of admitting damage.** "One Person State" as a title already does this — it sounds triumphant, sovereign, self-sufficient. But the best displacement writing admits that displacement *damages*. It doesn't just redirect you. It breaks things inside you that may never heal. The book must hold both: the sovereignty AND the wound. The system AND the nights when the system doesn't work. The new name AND the grief for the old one.

Alexievich's subjects never present themselves as systems. They say: *"I don't understand what happened to me."* The most powerful moment in this book will be the moment Ray says something similar — when the framework fails, when the pipeline reveals a person inside it, confused and hurt and far from home.

That is the book.

---

*Evaluation prepared in the tradition of displacement literature criticism. The material is extraordinary. The architecture is premature. Build the house from the wound outward, not from the blueprint downward.*
