## DAY 22 — March 30, Monday
**Pillar:** governance
**Format:** confession+framework

**POST:**
when you hate how things work, you have two options.

leave, or stay and fight to change it.

an economist named Albert Hirschman called these "exit" and "voice" in 1970.

exit is clean. you leave the company, the country, the relationship. you vote with your feet.

voice is messy. you complain, organize, protest, propose, negotiate. you stay and make noise until something changes.

most of us default to one or the other.

I'm an exit person. always have been. bad job? quit. bad city? move. bad system? build a new one.

## DAY 25 — April 2, Thursday
**Pillar:** AI+sovereignty
**Format:** contrarian take

**POST:**
a philosopher named Ivan Illich argued in 1973 that tools should serve people, not the other way around.

he called them "convivial tools" — tools that expand what a person can do without requiring dependence on experts or systems.

a bicycle is convivial. it makes you faster without making you dependent.

a car is not. it requires roads, fuel, insurance, mechanics, licenses, and an entire industrial system.

now look at AI through this lens.

is ChatGPT a bicycle or a car?

it makes you faster. but it requires internet, a subscription, a company's server, that company's policy decisions, and trust that they won't change the terms tomorrow.

the convivial version of AI would be: runs on your machine. owned by you. no subscription. no terms of service that change monthly. no company deciding what you're allowed to ask.

we're building the car version of AI and calling it liberation.

Illich would have had opinions.

day 25/30

what tool in your life feels like a bicycle, and which one feels like a car you can't escape?

**FIRST COMMENT:**
"Tools for Conviviality" by Ivan Illich, 1973. 53 years later and it reads like it was written about the AI debate. the man predicted platform dependency before platforms existed. free PDF exists online if you search for it.
