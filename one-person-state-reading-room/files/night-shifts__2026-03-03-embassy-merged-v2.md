last week I filled out a registration form for an Ivan Illich centenary gathering at a monastery in Tuscany. scholars, theologians, a few activists. standard fields — name, affiliation, country.

I wrote "network state."

sat with it for ten minutes before hitting submit. the honest answer — "I don't know anymore" — wouldn't fit in the field.

## the same crash, different triggers

I've rebuilt my identity across three countries. each time the thing that answered "where are you from?" and "what do you do?" got wiped clean. Belarus to Kyiv to Lisbon. by the third reset I'd stopped expecting the labels to stick.

you find out fast how much of your identity was actually yours when the place validating it disappears. the answer, in my case: less than I thought.

a friend is going through his own version right now. decade of backend experience. told me last week he feels like an intern. his junior colleague matches his output using Cursor. nobody moved countries. the skill that defined him just got repriced overnight.

same crash. the thing that labeled you is gone. what's underneath?

## the authorship gap

I co-authored [the context gap](https://gap.aimindset.org) earlier this year — ten loops where machine capability and human experience pull apart. loop 7 is the one that keeps coming back: authorship.

I DJ. played a set at a Belarusian spring-calling ritual in Lisbon last month. didn't compose a single track. the set was mine — selection, sequencing, reading the room. nobody asks a DJ "did you write this?" they ask if it landed. authorship lives in the curation, the timing, the calls.

code is heading the same direction. using AI well is its own skill — the debugging, the prompting, the knowing when to override. but skill and identity are different questions.

I wrote about the related angle in [a dead priest and your AI problem](https://embassy.svit.la/p/a-dead-priest-and-your-ai-problem) — can you still do the thing without the tool? that was about capability erosion. this is about something harder to measure. you might pass that test clean and still feel the ground shift as the market reprices your craft.

when labor gets cheaper, identity gets more expensive.

## the decision stack

your output is a commodity. your decision stack is not.

decision stack: what problem you chose, which constraints you accepted, which features you killed, which risks you personally own. the distance between "I made an app" and "I can tell you exactly why this app exists and what I'd change."

the model doesn't lose sleep when your product fails. you do. that asymmetry is authorship.

I [went from seven figures to insolvent](https://embassy.svit.la/p/fork-yourself-before-the-state) because I had output and no decisions underneath it. shipped everything, chose nothing. my identity was "the guy who ships." when shipping stopped working, there was nothing there.

## the one person state

Illich had a name for the space where you do things your own way, outside institutional permission: the vernacular domain. the monastery gathering is about his centenary — a hundred years later, the question is the same. can you name what's yours when the institutions stop naming it for you?

the [one person state](https://embassy.svit.la/p/the-ritual-kaleidoscope) I've been building in this newsletter grows from that idea and from my own wreckage. identity as infrastructure. rituals, decisions, config. something that holds whether the market prices your labor at $200/hour or $20. whether your country exists on a map or doesn't.

for people who've rebuilt themselves across countries and languages, this isn't new. authoring yourself from fragments is what diaspora builders already do. the decision stack is just a name for what they've been practicing.

## what this looks like on a tuesday

I journal my decisions. thirty seconds after each one. why this architecture. why I killed that feature. why I'm writing about a dead priest instead of making a LinkedIn carousel about AI productivity.

last week: killed the recommendation engine in the MVP. it needed user data I don't have and would train users to expect personalization I can't sustain. less engagement, cleaner onboarding. thirty seconds of writing. the one artifact from that build week no model can claim.

nobody pays you for this. it doesn't scale. it won't trend.

but it's the only practice that answered the question on the monastery form.

country: the decisions I've made and the corners I've chosen to stand in.
