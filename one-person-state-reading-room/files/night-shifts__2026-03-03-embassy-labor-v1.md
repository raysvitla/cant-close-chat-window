# when labor gets cheaper, identity gets more expensive

*loop 7 from the context gap*

---

You shipped the feature. Described it, set the constraints, caught the hallucination on line 47, rewrote the test yourself. Monday deploy. No incidents.

Someone on the team asks who wrote it.

You pause.

That pause is what this issue is about.

## the authorship gap

Earlier this year I co-authored a research piece called [The Context Gap](https://gap.aimindset.org) with the team at AI Mindset — ten loops where machine capability and human experience pull in opposite directions. Loop 7 keeps circling back to me.

The machine signal is clear: coding is now the first broadly proven agent category. AI systems write, refactor, test, ship. 92% of US developers were using AI coding tools by late 2025. The value is measurable and the ROI decks write themselves at this point — probably literally.

The human signal is quieter. Most developers I talk to love the tools. They ship faster, take on bigger scopes, build things they couldn't build alone. But underneath the productivity gain, a question is forming. If the machine did the structural work, what part of this is yours?

I tracked my own screen for sixteen days last month. Every prompt, every tab switch, every moment I reached for Claude instead of sitting with a problem. Day three I noticed a pattern: I was asking questions I already knew the answers to. By day nine, I'd stopped forming my own take before checking. I wasn't outsourcing the hard problems. I was outsourcing the easy ones — the thirty-second questions I could answer if I just sat with them. Trading attention for convenience. And the exchange rate was worse than I thought. Each shortcut made me trust my own judgment a little less.

That's the gap. When labor gets cheaper, identity gets more expensive.

## where authorship goes

I DJ. Last month I played a set at a Belarusian spring-calling ritual here in Lisbon — gukanne vyasny, the old tradition of singing spring into existence. I didn't compose a single track in that set. But the set was mine: the selection, the sequencing, the way I read the room and pivoted halfway through when the energy shifted. Nobody asks a DJ "did you write this music?" They ask whether the set landed. Authorship lives in the curation, the timing, the calls.

Code is heading the same direction. Has been for a while. AI just compressed the timeline from decades to months.

I wrote about a related angle in [a dead priest and your AI problem](https://embassy.svit.la/p/a-dead-priest-and-your-ai-problem) — Illich's watershed test. Can you still do the thing without the tool? That was about capability erosion. This is about something harder to measure. You might pass the watershed test clean and still feel the ground shift, because the craft you anchored your professional identity to — writing code, solving problems by typing solutions — is being repriced while you watch.

I vibe-code. I'm part of this, sort of. But I also called it in [the ritual kaleidoscope](https://embassy.svit.la/p/the-ritual-kaleidoscope): rootless practice doesn't survive inflation. The question isn't whether you used AI to build it. It's whether you can explain why you built what you built, what you cut, what you kept, which tradeoff you accepted and which one you refused.

That explanation is authorship now. It's the only part that's scarce.

## the decision stack

Your output is a commodity. Your decision stack is not.

Decision stack: what problem you chose to solve, which constraints you accepted, which features you killed, which risks you personally took on. The distance between "I made an app" and "I made this specific app for these specific reasons, and here's what I'd change next time."

AI can't own a decision stack. Ownership requires consequences. The model doesn't lose sleep when your users churn or your startup folds. You do. That's what makes authorship real — you have to live with the result.

I know what it's like when there's nothing underneath the output. Before AI was in the picture, I [built to seven figures and went insolvent](https://embassy.svit.la/p/fork-yourself-before-the-state) because I couldn't manage my own context switches. My identity was "the guy who ships." When shipping stopped working, I had no decision framework. No sovereignty over my own attention. Just output and a story about output.

The [one person state](https://embassy.svit.la/p/requiresovereign-individual-undefined) I've been building in this newsletter is partly a response to that collapse. Your identity has to be a system — rituals, tools, config — something that holds when the market reprices your labor overnight.

## the math

Stack Overflow traffic dropped 35% the year after ChatGPT launched. Junior developer postings in the US fell by half. The developers who stayed shipped 26% more code per quarter. A friend of mine — a decade of backend experience — told me last week he feels like an intern again, because his new junior colleague matches his output using Cursor.

Fewer people, more output, the main knowledge commons hollowing out. The labor got cheaper. The identity cost is just beginning to compound.

## what I'm doing about it

I journal my decisions. Why I chose this architecture. Why I killed that feature. Why I picked this market over the easier one. I'm building a log of my own judgment, so when output becomes indistinguishable from what any agent could produce, there's a trail of thinking that's mine.

Last week's entry: killed the recommendation engine in the MVP. Reason — it would require user data I don't have yet and would train users to expect personalization I can't sustain at launch. Tradeoff accepted: less engagement, cleaner onboarding. Thirty seconds of writing. And it's the one artifact from that build that no model can claim.

It doesn't ship. It doesn't demo. But it resolved the pause from the top of this piece.

Someone asks who wrote this.

You wrote it. You decided what should exist, and you're the one who answers when it breaks.
