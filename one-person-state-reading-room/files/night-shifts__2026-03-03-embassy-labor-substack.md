You shipped the feature. Described it, set the constraints, caught the hallucination on line 47, rewrote the test yourself. Monday deploy. No incidents.

Someone on the team asks who wrote it.

You pause.

## the authorship gap

Earlier this year I co-authored a research piece called [The Context Gap](https://gap.aimindset.org) with the team at AI Mindset — ten loops where machine capability and human experience pull apart. Loop 7 hasn't left me alone since.

The machine signal: coding is the first broadly proven agent category. AI writes, refactors, tests, ships. 92% of US developers using AI coding tools by late 2025. Fast enough that the tooling conversation is already boring.

The human signal is weirder. Developers love the tools. They also wonder what's left that's theirs.

I tracked my own screen for sixteen days last month. Every prompt, every tab switch. Day three: I caught myself asking Claude questions I already knew the answers to. Day nine: I'd stopped forming my own take before checking. I was handing off the easy problems. The thirty-second ones. Each time, it got harder to trust that I had a take worth having.

I still do this. I haven't fixed it. I see it happen and choose convenience anyway, most days.

That's the gap. When labor gets cheaper, identity gets more expensive.

## where authorship goes

I DJ. Played a set at a Belarusian spring-calling ritual in Lisbon last month — didn't compose a single track. The set was mine anyway: selection, sequencing, reading the room. Nobody asks a DJ "did you write this?" They ask if it landed. Code is going the same way, faster.

I wrote about the adjacent problem in [a dead priest and your AI problem](https://embassy.svit.la/p/a-dead-priest-and-your-ai-problem) — whether you can still do the thing without the tool. But capability and identity are different problems. You can pass that test and still feel the ground move as the market reprices your craft.

Using AI well is its own skill — the debugging, the prompting, the knowing when to override. I'm not dismissing that. I vibe-code, I ship with it, I'm in it. But I also called it in [the ritual kaleidoscope](https://embassy.svit.la/p/the-ritual-kaleidoscope): rootless practice doesn't survive inflation. Can you explain why you built what you built? What you cut? What you kept?

## the decision stack

Your output is a commodity. Your decision stack is yours.

What problem you chose. Which features you killed. Which constraints you accepted. Which risks you own. The distance between "I made an app" and "I can tell you exactly why this app exists and what I'd change."

The model doesn't lose sleep when your product fails. You do. That asymmetry is authorship.

I know what happens when there's nothing under the output. I [built to seven figures and went insolvent](https://embassy.svit.la/p/fork-yourself-before-the-state) because I couldn't manage my own context switches. My identity was "the guy who ships." When shipping stopped working, I had nothing underneath. No sovereignty over my attention. Just output and a story I told about it.

The [one person state](https://embassy.svit.la/p/requiresovereign-individual-undefined) I've been building in this newsletter grows from that wreckage. Identity as a system you maintain — rituals, tools, decisions, config. Something that holds whether the market values your labor at $200/hour or $20.

For people who've rebuilt their identity across countries and languages — a lot of my readers — this isn't new. Authoring yourself from fragments is something diaspora builders already do. The decision stack is just a name for what they've been practicing.

## the quiet part

A friend — decade of backend experience — told me last week he feels like an intern. His new junior colleague matches his output using Cursor. Stack Overflow's traffic collapsed 35% in a year. The infrastructure that let people teach themselves to code, thinning when more people need it.

Fewer people. More output. And the thing that used to connect craft to community, disappearing in the middle.

## what I'm doing about it

I journal my decisions. Why this architecture. Why I killed that feature. Why this market.

Last week: killed the recommendation engine in the MVP. It would need user data I don't have and would train users to expect personalization I can't sustain. Tradeoff: less engagement, cleaner onboarding. Thirty seconds of writing. The one artifact from that build that no model can claim.

This practice doesn't scale. It doesn't make content. Nobody will pay you for it. But it's the only thing that quieted the pause from the top of this piece.

Someone asks who wrote this.

You wrote it. You decided what should exist. You'll answer when it breaks.
