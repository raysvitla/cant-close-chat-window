Advisory Board Report — "One Person State" External Support Evaluation

Date: 2026-04-08

Subject: Ray Svitla

Question: What external support structure (residency / fellowship / grant / none) best serves the One Person State book, given Ray's current life constraints?

Context Snapshot

  • Book status: Mid-construction. Structure resolved at 2026-03-07 board session: "Three Resets" (Minsk → Kyiv → Lisbon) as visible scaffold, Illich philosophy hidden underneath. Body-as-territory is the surprise chapter. Confessional voice over framework voice. Emotional core: grief performed as engineering. Signature line: "they took my country three times, so I built one that fits inside my body."
  • Life: Full-time client work (Stay Insured). Lisbon, wife, cat. Planned move to Israel in ~12 months. NHR tax status (clock ticking on Portuguese residency value).
  • Parallel commitments: self.md daily radar, 404Embassy newsletter, Belarus Network State 2030, Illich academic chapter (6–8k words, abstract deadline already slipped, final due Dec 2026, Italy gathering June 11–18, 2026).

Expert 1 — The Red Team (Adversarial): "Don't Apply. Ship It."

Persona: A veteran independent writer who has watched a generation of friends apply themselves into paralysis. Has written four books, none on a residency. Believes the application industrial complex is a self-soothing loop for people who are afraid to finish.

Ray, listen carefully, because everyone else in this room is going to tell you to apply for something. I'm telling you: don't. Not now. Not this book.

Here's what an application actually costs you. Each serious residency or fellowship is 20–40 hours of prose: project description, writing sample, work plan, references, budget narrative, sometimes a video. That prose is written in framework voice — the exact voice your last board told you to abandon. You will spend the next three months translating your confessional book into institutional language to convince a committee that your grief is legible. That translation will infect the manuscript. I have seen it a dozen times. The writer comes back from the residency with a book that sounds like a grant application with chapter breaks.

Second: your book's entire thesis is one person state. Sovereignty inside the body. The surprise chapter is body-as-territory. You are going to ask an institution — a literal state-adjacent funding body — to legitimize a book whose central claim is that institutions took your country three times and you built one they can't touch? That is a performative contradiction you will feel in your teeth every morning at the residency desk.

Third: you already have the conditions most residencies simulate. You have a room. You have a wife who gets it. You have a cat. You have Lisbon light. You have a client that pays. You have an Illich gathering in Italy in June that is already the community a fellowship would pretend to give you. The only thing you don't have is permission, and permission is exactly what a fellowship sells at a 95% rejection rate.

What you actually need is 90 minutes a day, six days a week, for eight months. That is 360 hours. That is a first draft. You can steal that from Stay Insured mornings. You do not need Yaddo. You do not need Civitella. You need a chair and the willingness to disappoint yourself on the page before breakfast.

My vote: don't apply. Ship the book by the time you land in Tel Aviv. Let the Illich chapter be your only external deadline. The book is not a grant; it is a confession, and confessions do not get sponsored.

Expert 2 — The Grant Strategist (Practical Implementation): "Apply Surgically, Not Broadly"

Persona: Former program officer at a European writing foundation, now advises mid-career nonfiction writers on the residency/fellowship landscape. Reads 300 applications a year. Knows exactly which programs fit which life stages.

Ray, I hear the Red Team and I half-agree. Broad applying is a trap. But surgical applying — two to three carefully chosen targets that match your constraints — is a different game. Let me walk you through the actual landscape for someone in your exact position.

What disqualifies most programs for you right now: residencies requiring 4+ weeks on-site (incompatible with Stay Insured), US-based programs (visa friction, and you're moving east not west), programs requiring unpublished writers (you're not), programs that pay in prestige not money (you have NHR; you need cash-equivalent value or time, not a line on a bio).

What fits your shape:

  1. Non-residential fellowships / project grants. These give money and a deadline without making you leave Lisbon. Look at: Logan Nonfiction Program's remote track, Creative Capital (if you can frame the book as hybrid), Sloan (if you lean the Illich/technology angle), and European-specific ones like DAAD's non-residential options or the Allianz Kulturstiftung project grants. The Dutch Foundation for Literature has translation/development grants that accept non-Dutch writers in certain categories.
  2. Short residencies (7–14 days) stackable with existing travel. Since you're already going to Italy June 11–18 for the Illich gathering, look for a 1-week residency in Umbria or Tuscany the week after. Santa Maddalena Foundation (Tuscany) is exactly this shape — short, no bureaucracy, serious writers, and geographically aligned.
  3. The Israel bridge move. Since you're moving in ~12 months, Mishkenot Sha'ananim in Jerusalem and the Jerusalem Writers Festival fellowship are worth a scouting application. Getting accepted after you arrive turns your relocation into a soft-landing infrastructure. Apply now, aim for a 2027 slot.

What I would NOT do: I would not apply to anything that requires a finished manuscript in 2026. I would not apply to anything requiring more than 10 days away from wife/cat/client in the next 8 months. I would not apply to anything that asks you to pretend this is a policy book — it isn't.

Realistic hit rate: If you apply to 3 well-matched programs, expect 1 acceptance. Total application labor: ~40 hours, compressible into two weekends. ROI is favorable only if you pick programs whose deadlines are themselves the product — i.e., the external deadline is worth more than the money.

My vote: apply to exactly 3 things, chosen for deadline pressure and Israel bridge value, not prestige.

Expert 3 — The Life-Design Voice (Compatibility with Wife / Israel / Client): "The Move Is the Residency"

Persona: A life-design coach who works with immigrant creative professionals. Thinks in terms of life seasons, not projects. Has seen a lot of marriages strained by "I just need six weeks away to finish the thing."

Let me zoom out from the book for a second and look at the next 18 months of Ray's actual life.

You are in the final year of a chapter. Lisbon is closing. NHR is closing. Your wife has been through three countries with you and is about to do a fourth. There is a cat involved, which is not a joke — cats are load-bearing infrastructure in a marriage during relocation. You have a full-time client relationship that pays for all of this and cannot be casually paused. And somewhere in this, you want to write a book whose emotional core is grief performed as engineering.

Here is the life-design reality: the Israel move IS the residency. You are about to have a 3–6 month window of forced interiority. New apartment, bureaucracy, no friends yet, jet-lagged mornings, Hebrew you don't speak, a wife also in transition, and — critically — a hard narrative break from Lisbon that maps exactly onto the "Three Resets" structure of your book. The book's architecture is about resets. You are about to perform a fourth reset while writing about the first three. That is not a problem. That is the most valuable writing condition a nonfiction author can have, and no fellowship can manufacture it.

So the question becomes: what do you need to protect that window, not replace it?

  1. Do not add a residency that requires leaving your wife during relocation prep. A 4-week stint in Italy in October 2026 while she's packing boxes alone is how marriages get quietly damaged. This is non-negotiable.
  2. The June Italy gathering (Illich) is already your one permitted away-trip for 2026. Treat it as sacred. Don't stack a second long absence.
  3. Client work is not the enemy of this book. It's the ground. Stay Insured mornings fund the cat, the wife, the move, and the NHR window. Quitting to write full-time would add financial pressure that would show up in the prose as panic. Keep the client.
  4. What you actually need from any external structure: something that gives you a deadline in the first 90 days after landing in Israel, not before. Something that says "first draft by [date 3 months post-arrival]." That turns the disorientation of the move into fuel.

My vote: don't apply to anything that lives inside 2026. Apply only to things with Israel-side 2027 deadlines. Let the move be the residency.

Expert 4 — The Writer-Craft Voice (What the Book Needs): "A Reader, Not a Room"

Persona: A nonfiction editor who has shepherded memoirs with hybrid philosophical frames. Thinks diagnostically: every book at every stage needs a specific thing, and getting the wrong thing is worse than getting nothing.

Let me ask the only question that matters: what does this book, specifically, lack right now?

Not books in general. Not writers in general. This book. This writer. Let's diagnose.

Does it lack time? Partially, but not catastrophically. 90 minutes a day gets you there in eight months. Ray already has that time; he just hasn't locked it.

Does it lack money? No. NHR + Stay Insured covers the runway. A $15k fellowship would be nice but wouldn't change the manuscript.

Does it lack community? Arguably — but the Illich Italy gathering in June is already the highest-quality community this book could possibly land in. A residency full of novelists would actively dilute the intellectual environment. Ray needs Illich scholars and former-Soviet-space thinkers, not generalist MFA alumni.

Does it lack deadline pressure? Yes — but the Illich academic chapter (6–8k words, due December 2026) is already that. Ray can use the Illich chapter as a forcing function: writing the chapter feeds the book's hidden philosophical layer. Those 6–8k words are not separate from the book; they are the book's subterranean thesis rehearsed in academic register. Treat them as one project with two outputs.

Does it lack a room? No. Lisbon apartment, wife-supported, cat-adjacent. Rooms are not the bottleneck.

So what does it actually lack? One serious reader who can hold the whole manuscript in their head and push back on the confessional voice when it flinches. That is the single missing piece. Not a residency. Not a grant. An editor, or a developmental reader, or one ferociously honest writer-friend who will read 40 pages a month and say "this is where you went back into framework voice, go again."

This is a $1,500–$4,000 hire, not a $15,000 fellowship. It's purchasable today. It requires no application. It can start next week. And it addresses the one actual craft risk in this book — that the confessional voice will flinch and retreat into framework voice under the pressure of finishing alone.

Bonus: a reader who also knows the Illich literature could double as feedback on the academic chapter. That's a two-birds hire.

My vote: don't apply. Hire a reader. The book needs a witness, not a room.

Synthesis

Consensus (3 of 4, with Grant Strategist partially aligned)

  1. Broad applying is wrong for this book right now. All four experts reject a wide application campaign. The Red Team and Life-Design voices reject it outright; the Grant Strategist allows only 2–3 surgical applications; the Writer-Craft voice sees no craft need a residency would solve.
  2. The June 2026 Illich gathering in Italy is already the right community event and should not be stacked with additional long absences.
  3. Stay Insured client work should continue — it's the ground, not the enemy. Financial pressure would damage the prose.
  4. The Illich academic chapter and the book are one project, not two. The 6–8k words rehearse the hidden philosophical layer of One Person State.
  5. The Israel move is a structural event for the book, not a disruption to work around.

Disagreements

  • Red Team vs Grant Strategist on whether to apply at all. Red Team: zero applications. Grant Strategist: three surgical applications, chosen for deadline value and Israel bridge.
  • Grant Strategist vs Writer-Craft on what's missing. Grant Strategist sees deadline pressure as the external gift; Writer-Craft sees a developmental reader as the missing piece and considers deadlines already covered by the Illich chapter.
  • Life-Design vs Grant Strategist on timing. Life-Design says nothing in 2026; Grant Strategist says a short Tuscany residency adjacent to the Illich gathering is fine.

Top 3 Action Items

  1. Hire a developmental reader by end of April 2026. Budget $2–4k. Priority: someone who knows Illich literature and can hold both the book manuscript and the academic chapter in one head. This is the highest-leverage move, requires no application, and directly addresses the confessional-voice flinch risk. (Writer-Craft's recommendation, unopposed by other experts.)
  2. Treat the Illich academic chapter as the book's forcing function. Write the abstract this week (already slipped), lock December 2026 as the non-negotiable deliverable, and treat those 6–8k words as the subterranean philosophical spine rehearsal. The Italy gathering June 11–18 is the one sanctioned away-trip of 2026. (Consensus across all four experts.)
  3. Scout — do not apply to — 2027 Israel-side opportunities now. Specifically: Mishkenot Sha'ananim (Jerusalem) and Jerusalem Writers Festival fellowship. Build a file, note deadlines, do not submit until Q4 2026 when the Israel move is concrete. This turns the relocation into soft-landing infrastructure without consuming 2026 application labor. (Grant Strategist + Life-Design alignment.)

ONE Clear Recommendation: DON'T APPLY (with one narrow exception)

Recommendation: Do not apply to residencies or fellowships in 2026.

The three strongest voices in the room (Red Team, Life-Design, Writer-Craft) converge on the same conclusion from different angles: this book's bottleneck is not time, not money, not community, and not a room. It is a developmental reader who can guard the confessional voice against flinching into framework voice, and a deadline that is already supplied by the Illich academic chapter. An application campaign in 2026 would cost 20–120 hours of framework-voice prose, strain the marriage during relocation prep, and address no actual craft deficit.

Narrow exception: Scout 2–3 Israel-based programs (Mishkenot Sha'ananim, Jerusalem Writers Festival) for 2027 deadlines only, to be submitted from Israel after arrival. Treat these as soft-landing infrastructure, not as 2026 projects.

What to do instead with the next 90 days:

  • Week 1: Send the overdue Illich abstract. Stop the slippage.
  • Week 2: Hire a developmental reader ($2–4k). One page of criteria, three candidate conversations, pick one.
  • Week 3: Lock 90 minutes per day, six days a week, pre-Stay-Insured, as book time. Non-negotiable.
  • Weeks 4–12: First 30k words of manuscript + Illich chapter draft, both reviewed monthly by the reader.

The signature line of the book already answers the board's question. Ray wrote: "they took my country three times, so I built one that fits inside my body." A book whose thesis is internal sovereignty cannot be externally sponsored without a tonal contradiction the author will feel on every page. The One Person State ships itself, or it isn't what it claims to be.

End of report.
