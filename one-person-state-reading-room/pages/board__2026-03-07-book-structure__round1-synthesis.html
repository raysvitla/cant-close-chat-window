consensus (all 5 agree)

  1. Illich is the intellectual spine. Every expert identifies Illich as the strongest, most novel contribution. Not a chapter — the foundation.
  2. The autobiography must be structural, not decorative. Ray's life is the argument. Topic-organized chapters = essay collection, not book.
  3. Chapters 5 (Phantom States) and 9 (Swarm of One) are the weakest. Research-interest chapters that don't serve the reader. Cut or compress.
  4. Body as territory is the surprise chapter. Nobody in the network state discourse has done this. The most memorable, unexpected contribution.
  5. "Shipped everything, chose nothing" and "portable because it's mine" are the book's voice at its best. The confessional-specific moments beat the framework moments.

emerging structure (convergence of all 5)

All experts gravitate toward some version of: autobiography as spine, Illich as intellectual engine, stack logic as hidden architecture. The disagreement is about emphasis (more memoir? more philosophy? more practical?).

A merged structure might look like:

Part I — what was lost (Minsk, the first stripping)

Part II — what was built and lost again (Kyiv, WAKA, the institutional trap)

Part III — what was found (Lisbon, the body, the configuration, the practice)

With Illich threaded throughout (not a single chapter but a returning voice), and the sovereignty stack quietly building from body→sound→mind→tools→ritual→territory.
