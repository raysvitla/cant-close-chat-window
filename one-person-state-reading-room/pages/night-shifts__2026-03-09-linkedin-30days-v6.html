DAY 1 — March 9, Monday

Pillar: founder lessons

Format: confession+framework

POST:

I lost a 7-figure net worth in 18 months.

not to a bad investment. not to a market crash.

to context switching.

I was running 7 projects simultaneously. consulting. research. a startup. a newsletter. a DJ project. two more I've already forgotten the names of.

every Monday I'd convince myself I was making progress on all of them. every Friday I'd realize I'd made progress on none.

the math is brutal: 7 projects × 3 hours each = 21 hours a week that feel productive. but the switching cost between them eats another 15. so you're left with 6 hours of actual deep work. spread across 7 things.

that's 51 minutes per project per week.

try building anything meaningful in 51 minutes a week.

I now run a strict constraint: 3 nodes maximum. three active projects. everything else goes on a waiting list or dies.

it's not a productivity hack. it's damage control for a brain that wants to do everything.

DAY 6 — March 14, Saturday

Pillar: governance

Format: contrarian take

POST:

800 billion lines of COBOL still run the global banking system.

a language from 1959. older than the moon landing. and it processes 95% of ATM transactions and 80% of in-person transactions worldwide.

we don't replace it because replacing it is terrifying. the cost of failure is the entire economy.

now think about governance the same way.

the systems that run your country — voting, taxation, legislation, courts — are the COBOL of civilization. ancient. fragile. and so deeply embedded that nobody dares touch them.

not because they work well.

because the downside of a bad upgrade is catastrophic.

so we keep patching. adding layers on top of layers. building workarounds for workarounds.

sound familiar? it should. every enterprise architect in your feed just nodded.

the question isn't whether we need to upgrade the rules that run society.

the question is: how do you refactor a system you can't take offline?

DAY 7 — March 15, Sunday

Pillar: governance

Format: contrarian take

POST:

you signed a Terms of Service you never read when you were born.

it's called the social contract.

it dictates where you can work. how much of your income you keep. what substances you can put in your body. who you can marry in some places. what you can say in others.

you didn't agree to it. you can't fork it. there's no competitor offering better terms.

the only opt-out is moving to a different country — which requires permission from both the country you're leaving and the country you're entering.

imagine if any SaaS product operated this way.

"you can cancel your subscription, but only if a competing product approves your transfer, and also you need to physically relocate your entire life."

the App Store would reject it.

but somehow it's the foundation of modern civilization.

I'm not saying this to be nihilistic. I'm saying it because the first step to building better systems is admitting the current ones are weird.

DAY 22 — March 30, Monday

Pillar: governance

Format: confession+framework

POST:

when you hate how things work, you have two options.

leave, or stay and fight to change it.

an economist named Albert Hirschman called these "exit" and "voice" in 1970.

exit is clean. you leave the company, the country, the relationship. you vote with your feet.

voice is messy. you complain, organize, protest, propose, negotiate. you stay and make noise until something changes.

most of us default to one or the other.

I'm an exit person. always have been. bad job? quit. bad city? move. bad system? build a new one.

404Embassy is the most "exit" project possible — literally building alternative governance structures.

but here's what 21 days of writing in public taught me: you can't build an alternative without voice. without explaining, persuading, arguing, listening.

pure exit is just running away with a business plan.

voice is what makes exit meaningful.

day 22/30

are you more of an exit person or a voice person — and has that served you well?

FIRST COMMENT:

Hirschman's book "Exit, Voice, and Loyalty" is 55 years old and still the best framework for understanding why people leave organizations (or countries). if you've ever rage-quit a job, you've lived this theory. worth a read.

5 things I learned about founding teams by being on terrible ones.

DAY 25 — April 2, Thursday

Pillar: AI+sovereignty

Format: contrarian take

POST:

a philosopher named Ivan Illich argued in 1973 that tools should serve people, not the other way around.

he called them "convivial tools" — tools that expand what a person can do without requiring dependence on experts or systems.

a bicycle is convivial. it makes you faster without making you dependent.

a car is not. it requires roads, fuel, insurance, mechanics, licenses, and an entire industrial system.

now look at AI through this lens.

is ChatGPT a bicycle or a car?

it makes you faster. but it requires internet, a subscription, a company's server, that company's policy decisions, and trust that they won't change the terms tomorrow.

the convivial version of AI would be: runs on your machine. owned by you. no subscription. no terms of service that change monthly. no company deciding what you're allowed to ask.

we're building the car version of AI and calling it liberation.

Illich would have had opinions.
