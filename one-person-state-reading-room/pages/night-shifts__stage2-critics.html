STAGE 2: FIVE CRITICS

Critic 1: Ray's wife

"So this is good. But — isn't this just privilege? You rebuilt your identity three times, sure. You also had savings, a working passport, and a fellowship. Most people who flee Kyiv don't end up writing newsletters from Lisbon about monasteries in Tuscany. You need to say that, not because it undermines your point, but because not saying it makes you sound oblivious.

Also — 'Build that' at the end? Build what? It's a motivational poster. You told a real story and then ended with a bumper sticker. What's the actual takeaway for someone who hasn't moved countries three times?

And 'you don't discover your actual identity until everything gets stripped away' — that's a pretty romantic way to describe what was, for you, traumatic. Don't dress it up."

Actionable:

  • Acknowledge the privilege directly — "I got to Lisbon, I had savings and a passport that worked"
  • Kill "Build that." — end with something concrete and honest
  • Don't romanticize the stripping; show that the answer was "less than I thought"

Critic 3: The slop detector

Flagged AI-sounding sentences:

  1. "identity as infrastructure you carry" — borderline corporate metaphor, could pass as Ray's voice given dev context
  2. "the thing that holds value is the practice underneath. The rituals. The config. The decision stack." — fragment list stacking, classic AI emphasis pattern
  3. "Decisions. Taste. The weird intersections you've accumulated that no one else has." — same fragment list pattern, repeated
  4. "The most expensive thing you own isn't your skills. It's not your output. It's the thing that survives when..." — triple-beat negation building to reveal. AI cadence.
  5. "Build that." — motivational one-liner. Slop.
  6. "the real question on every form now is: what do you stand on when the label disappears?" — too neat, too essayistic
  7. "Not 'where are you from' but 'what do you decide and why.'" — uses the "not X — Y" structure flagged in style guide

Actionable:

  • Kill all fragment lists or fold them into actual sentences
  • Replace the "not X but Y" construction
  • Kill "Build that."
  • Rewrite the triple-beat ending — make it specific, not rhetorical

Critic 5: The Sajay (Illich scholar)

"The fact that Sajay Samuel reached out is a meaningful data point — it suggests the piece touched something real about Illich's project. But this newsletter doesn't go far enough with that connection.

Illich's core argument was about institutional monopolies stripping people of their vernacular competence — the ability to do things for yourself, in your own way, outside professional/institutional frameworks. The 'one person state' concept has a direct lineage to Illich's vernacular domain. That connection should be made explicit, even in one sentence. Otherwise it's name-dropping rather than building on the tradition.

The triple reset is genuinely interesting through an Illichian lens: each reset forced Ray out of an institutional identity (citizen, founder, member of a national tech scene) and back into vernacular selfhood. That's not just a personal story — it's a structural argument about what happens when institutions fail.

'Dead priest' as shorthand — I understand the rhetorical function, but Illich was arguably the most important critic of institutional modernity in the 20th century. The reduction has limits.

I would read this piece. I would not forward it to colleagues unless the Illich connection were deeper by one or two sentences."

Actionable:

  • Add one sentence connecting "one person state" to Illich's vernacular domain explicitly
  • Frame the triple reset as being forced OUT of institutional identity (not just moving countries)
  • Keep "dead priest" — it works for the audience — but add one beat of actual substance
