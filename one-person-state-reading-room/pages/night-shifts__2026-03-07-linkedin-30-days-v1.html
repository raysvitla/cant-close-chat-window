🏛️ BOARD OF DIRECTORS — LinkedIn Adaptation Analysis

SARAH — LinkedIn Algorithm Specialist

key findings:

  1. hook optimization: LinkedIn's "see more" fold hits after ~210 characters on desktop, ~150 on mobile. every post MUST have its strongest line in those first characters. the Threads hooks are strong but need restructuring for single-post format — no "1/5" numbering to signal "there's more."
  1. post length sweet spot: 1200-1800 characters gets optimal dwell time. 5-post threads need significant compression. singles need slight expansion. under 500 chars risks low reach (not enough dwell time). over 2000 chars risks drop-off.
  1. white space is currency: line breaks after every 1-2 sentences. single-line paragraphs. the algorithm tracks dwell time — more scrolling = more engagement signals. the Threads reply-chain format naturally had white space; LinkedIn needs it manually.
  1. hashtag strategy: 3-5 per post. mix of reach hashtags (#AI #leadership ~500M followers) with niche (#networkstates #sovereignty ~50K-200K). avoid over-broad (#business, #life). rotate to avoid looking formulaic.
  1. links ALWAYS in first comment: posts with external links get ~40% less reach. every post's link goes in the first comment. frame the comment as "the full essay →" to drive clicks.
  1. posting schedule: for 30-day campaign posting daily, Tue-Thu gets highest reach. Mon/Fri moderate. weekends lower reach but lower competition — good for personal/reflective posts. optimal time: 9-10am CET hits EU morning + US East Coast will see in their 6-7am scroll.
  1. first hour is critical: the algorithm evaluates engagement velocity in the first 60 minutes. Ray needs to actively reply to every comment in that window.

recommendation: compress threads to 1200-1800 chars. ensure hook is under 150 chars. use aggressive white space. end with implicit questions to drive comments.

DEREK — B2B Thought Leadership Strategist

key findings:

  1. unique positioning: Ray sits at a rare intersection — tech founder + governance theorist + diaspora voice + DJ. this multi-layered identity IS the brand. on LinkedIn, where everyone positions as "one thing," being genuinely multi-dimensional is a differentiator.
  1. credibility without credential-parade: the source material embeds credibility through EXPERIENCE (insolvency, 3-country rebuild, real product decisions) rather than titles. this is ideal for LinkedIn because the platform is oversaturated with "as a [title] at [company]..." openings.
  1. the lowercase risk/reward: bold move on LinkedIn. it will immediately differentiate from "5 Things I Learned About Leadership" posts. ~20% of readers will be put off. the remaining 80% will remember Ray. that's the right trade.
  1. series coherence: 30 consecutive days builds narrative momentum. the source material naturally arcs: personal sovereignty (Days 1-5) → governance theory (Days 6-12) → applied examples (Days 13-20) → synthesis (Days 21-30). maintain this arc.
  1. risk zone: governance OS comparisons and Pantheon analysis may read as "newsletter excerpts" without personal anchoring. every post needs a "why I personally care" moment.

recommendation: lead with personal stories, let theory emerge from lived experience. don't soften the lowercase — own it. add 1-2 posts that reference the LinkedIn context itself (the meta-play works here).

NINA — Creator Economy / Personal Brand Expert

key findings:

  1. authenticity advantage: raw, lowercase, vulnerable content will stand out on LinkedIn BECAUSE it breaks every LinkedIn content rule. the platform is starving for authentic voices amid the sea of "I'm humbled to announce" and inspirational sunrise photos.
  1. engagement magnets (in order of strength):

- insolvency story (Day 1) — vulnerability + expertise = instant trust

- monastery form story (Day 18) — identity crisis in real-time

- wife's question about the product (Day 28) — domestic truth > thought leadership

- father-in-law website (Day 30) — raw, unjustifiable emotion

- DJ set at Belarusian ritual (Day 14) — unexpected identity reveal

  1. theory posts need personal anchoring: governance OS (Day 2), runtime war (Day 19), Oracle suite metaphor (Day 26) — clever but cold. each needs a personal "why I care" moment in the first 3 lines.
  1. 30-day consistency signal: posting daily for 30 days tells the LinkedIn algorithm "this is a serious creator." profile visits and follower growth typically spike around days 7-10 of consistent posting.
  1. community building opportunity: the "one person state" concept can become a rallying identity for followers. people want to belong to something. the newsletter is the product; the LinkedIn series is the front door.

recommendation: always start with "I" or a personal detail before theory. preserve wife interactions at full length. end posts with implicit invitations for response. consider a meta-post framing the 30-day experiment.

TOMÁS — Tech/Web3 LinkedIn Niche Specialist

key findings:

  1. underserved niche: LinkedIn's Web3/governance audience is significant but starved for quality content. most Web3 discussion is on X/Farcaster. posting governance/network state content on LinkedIn reaches a different, more professional audience with virtually no competition.
  1. dev metaphors are LinkedIn gold: git init, vendor lock-in, kernel panics, /dev/null, API, fork — these make abstract governance concepts instantly graspable for the tech audience that dominates LinkedIn. the "governance stack" and "require('sovereign-individual')" framings are particularly strong.
  1. data credibility: specific numbers ($18.4B DAO treasuries, $525M Praxis, 22% trust, 83% MIT study) perform exceptionally well on LinkedIn. the platform's audience likes evidence-based arguments. keep ALL data points from the source.
  1. accessibility gap: some Web3 jargon (DAO, on-chain dispute resolution, proof-of-humanity) needs inline explanation. LinkedIn audience is broader than Threads/X crypto audience. a sentence of context saves losing non-crypto readers.
  1. the "governance as technology" frame: this is Ray's unique contribution. nobody else on LinkedIn is framing governance as a software engineering problem with this level of specificity. it's the killer positioning.

recommendation: lean into "governance as technology" framing. keep all data points. explain Web3 terms inline. the dev metaphors ARE the adaptation strategy — they make this content native to LinkedIn's tech audience.

LENA — Content Repurposing Specialist (Threads→LinkedIn)

key findings:

  1. structural transformation: Threads threads (1/5, 2/5...) become ONE flowing post. this requires: removing numbering, finding the through-line, cutting repetition between reply-posts, making transitions invisible.
  1. compression targets:

- 5-post threads → 1500-1800 chars (cut ~40%)

- 4-post threads → 1200-1500 chars (cut ~30%)

- 3-post threads → 1000-1200 chars (cut ~20%)

- singles → 500-900 chars (may expand slightly)

  1. what to preserve vs cut:

- PRESERVE: personal stories, wife conversations, data points, provocative conclusions

- CUT: transitional filler between thread posts, redundant restatements, secondary examples

- RESTRUCTURE: hooks may need to combine best elements from posts 1 and 2

  1. the emotional/theoretical balance: each LinkedIn post should follow the pattern: personal hook → theoretical insight → specific evidence → provocative conclusion. the Threads format allowed spreading this across 5 posts; LinkedIn needs it in one smooth arc.
  1. link placement: Threads link-in-last-post maps perfectly to LinkedIn link-in-first-comment. frame the comment as an invitation ("the full essay →") rather than a hard sell.
  1. platform voice calibration: Threads allows more experimental, fragment-style writing. LinkedIn rewards complete thoughts with clear takeaways. each post should have ONE shareable sentence — the line someone screenshots for their story.

recommendation: choose the strongest ~60% of each thread. preserve emotional beats at full length. compress theoretical sections. ensure one clear takeaway per post. the link-in-comment is a natural fit.

Ivan Illich defined a convivial tool as one passing four tests:
