The Junk Republic

A Conversation That Never Happened — Between Johan Huizinga and Carl Theodor Sørensen

Domain: Детские игры / Playground Design

Method: Воображаемый разговор между двумя мыслителями

Date: March 30, 2026

Copenhagen, autumn 1943. A café near Nørreport Station. The city is under German occupation. Johan Huizinga, the Dutch historian and author of "Homo Ludens" (1938), has been smuggled across the border by friends in the Danish resistance. He is frail — he will die in 1945, under house arrest in the Netherlands. Carl Theodor Sørensen, the Danish landscape architect, has just opened the world's first junk playground in Emdrup, on the grounds of a workers' housing cooperative. He is 50, energetic, troubled by the occupation but electrified by what he has seen the children do with his experiment.

They have been introduced by a mutual acquaintance in the Froebel Society. Over ersatz coffee and the sound of German trucks outside, they talk.

SØRENSEN: You must think it strange, Professor — a landscape architect who has given up on designing. I spent years building beautiful playgrounds. Sandboxes with perfect geometry. And do you know what I discovered?

HUIZINGA: That the children preferred the construction sites next door.

SØRENSEN: (pauses) You've seen this too.

HUIZINGA: I haven't seen your playground, Herr Sørensen. But I've spent my life watching what play is, and one thing I know with certainty: play cannot be imposed. The moment you design the play — prescribe the swinging, mandate the sliding — it ceases to be play at all. It becomes exercise. Drill. The gym class of the soul.

SØRENSEN: That's exactly what I found. I would build these elegant sandpits, these climbing frames, and the children would look at them with the dead eyes of museum visitors. But then they would escape — to the junkyards, to the rubble, to the places where things were broken and unfinished. There they came alive.

HUIZINGA: Because play requires a voluntary entry into a bounded space with rules that the player accepts freely. What you describe — the prescribed playground — is a space with rules imposed from outside. There is no magic circle.

SØRENSEN: Magic circle?

HUIZINGA: It is my term for the sacred precinct of play. The chalk line on the street. The edge of the tennis court. The threshold of the temple. Inside this boundary, different rules apply. Normal life is suspended. The player enters a temporary world within the ordinary world, dedicated to an activity with its own tendencies. This is the toverking — the magic circle. Every game has one. Every ritual has one. And I would argue — every sovereignty begins as one.

SØRENSEN: (leaning forward) Say more about that.

HUIZINGA: Consider. What is a jurisdiction? It is a bounded space within which certain rules apply, rules that are different from the rules outside. You cross a line — suddenly different laws govern you. You can do things here that you cannot do there. This is the magic circle of the state. And how did it begin? Not with constitutions and treaties. It began with the sacred grove. The market square. The playing field. Places where people agreed to enter different rules, voluntarily. The state is — forgive the provocation — a very old game.

SØRENSEN: Then what I've done in Emdrup...

HUIZINGA: What have you done there? Tell me precisely.

SØRENSEN: I enclosed a space — about 65 meters by 82 meters. I planted rosebushes and thornapple along the fence, partly to screen it from the street. Inside, I put nothing. No structures. Only materials: scrap wood, old tires, discarded metal, tools. Hammers. Saws. Nails. And I hired a pædagog — John Bertelsen, a remarkable man — not to teach, but to be present. To watch. To intervene only in extremis.

HUIZINGA: And what happened?

SØRENSEN: The children built a world.

HUIZINGA: (quietly) Of course they did.

SØRENSEN: They dug holes underground. They constructed houses on stilts. They built shacks and then knocked them down and rebuilt them differently. They made bonfires and cooked food. Bertelsen coined a word for it — skrammologi. Junkology. The science of building from waste.

HUIZINGA: And the rules?

SØRENSEN: That is the extraordinary thing. Nobody told them the rules. They made their own. Who can build where. Who gets the good planks. What happens when someone destroys another child's construction. How to share the fire. Bertelsen observed that the children developed what he called "a constitution of play" — unwritten, evolving, but real. Enforced not by adults but by the children themselves.

HUIZINGA: (nodding slowly) You have, without intending it, created a republic.

SØRENSEN: That troubles me. I am a landscape architect, not a political theorist.

HUIZINGA: But you have stumbled onto something that I have been trying to articulate for years. Play is not a consequence of culture. Play is not what culture produces when it has leisure. Play precedes culture. Play generates culture. As I wrote — "play is older than culture, for culture, however inadequately defined, always presupposes human society, and animals have not waited for man to teach them their playing."

SØRENSEN: You mean the children in Emdrup aren't imitating adult society...

HUIZINGA: They are inventing society. From scratch. As humans have always done. Every child on your junk playground is recapitulating the fundamental act of civilization: creating a bounded space, agreeing on rules, building structures, negotiating power. The fact that they do it with scrap wood and bent nails rather than marble and gold is irrelevant. The architecture of sovereignty is not made of materials. It is made of agreements.

SØRENSEN: Bertelsen writes in his diary that the playground is "an attempt to give the city child a substitute for the play and development potential it has lost." But now I wonder if it's more than that.

HUIZINGA: It is not a substitute. It is the original. The adult city — with its property laws and building codes and police — is the derived form. A calcified, bureaucratized version of what children do naturally when you give them a fence, some junk, and permission. Your playground is not a model of the city. The city is a decayed playground.

SØRENSEN: (laughing despite himself) I should not tell the Copenhagen planning department you said that.

HUIZINGA: They would not understand. They believe that order comes from planning. That someone must design the city, allocate the plots, prescribe the uses. But watch your children in Emdrup. Do they have a city planner?

SØRENSEN: No.

HUIZINGA: Do they have zoning laws?

SØRENSEN: Not in any formal sense.

HUIZINGA: And yet they produce neighborhoods. They produce territories. They produce hierarchies and alliances and trade agreements — "I'll give you nails if you let me use your ladder." They produce law, Professor Sørensen. Not written law, but the oldest law there is: customary law. Law that emerges from practice.

SØRENSEN: Piaget writes about this. I read him before starting the project. He says children pass through stages of moral development — first, they see rules as handed down by authority, immutable, like commandments from God. He calls this "heteronomous morality." But then, around ten or eleven, something shifts. They realize that rules are made by people. That rules can be changed — by mutual agreement, by negotiation, by play. He calls this "autonomous morality." The morality of cooperation.

HUIZINGA: And what is the transition point? When does a child move from obedience to autonomy?

SØRENSEN: Games. Specifically, games where children make their own rules. Piaget observed children playing marbles and found that the moment they understood they could modify the rules — as long as all players agreed — was the moment they became, in his term, "moral legislators."

HUIZINGA: Moral legislators. Not subjects. Not citizens. Legislators. Makers of law. This is the deep lesson of your junk playground. You are not raising citizens. You are raising founders.

SØRENSEN: There is a political dimension here that makes me uneasy, given what is happening outside this café.

HUIZINGA: You mean the Germans.

SØRENSEN: The playground opened because parents in the Emdrup housing cooperative were frightened. Their children were playing in the streets, in the ruins, and the parents feared that the occupation soldiers would mistake the children's play for sabotage. A child building a barricade from scrap might be shot. So the playground was, in part, a sanctuary. A place where play could happen without being mistaken for resistance.

HUIZINGA: But it is resistance. Every act of free play under occupation is resistance. The Germans know this. Why do you think totalitarian regimes organize youth into state-directed movements? The Hitler Youth, the Komsomol — these are anti-play. They take the natural impulse of young people to form groups, make rules, build things — and they channel it into obedience. They replace the magic circle, which is entered freely, with the parade ground, which is entered by command.

SØRENSEN: So the junk playground is a political act?

HUIZINGA: The most fundamental political act there is. It is the creation of a space of freedom within a space of unfreedom. This is not metaphor, Herr Sørensen. This is the literal architecture of every liberation movement. Every revolution begins with someone drawing a boundary and saying: "Within this line, we play by different rules."

SØRENSEN: I want to push you on something. You speak of the magic circle as voluntary. But the children in Emdrup — they don't all get along. There are conflicts. Stronger children dominate. Some children are excluded. Bertelsen sometimes has to intervene to prevent cruelty.

HUIZINGA: Of course. Play is not utopia. Play contains competition, aggression, even violence. The Greek agōn — the contest — is a form of play. War was once played by very formal rules. But here is the critical distinction: in genuine play, the conflict operates within a framework of mutual recognition. Even the fiercest competitors must agree on the rules of the game. The moment one player refuses all rules, the game collapses. There is a word for the player who breaks all rules — the spoilsport. And the spoilsport is more dangerous to the game than the cheat, because the cheat at least pretends to follow the rules.

SØRENSEN: And in a state?

HUIZINGA: The spoilsport is the tyrant. The one who says: "Rules do not apply to me." The cheat is the corrupt politician — despicable, but operating within the system. The tyrant destroys the game itself. This is what is happening in Europe now. The magic circle of civilization has been broken. The spoilsports are in charge.

SØRENSEN: Then the playground is a rehearsal for...

HUIZINGA: For the reconstruction. When this war ends — and it will end — the task will be to rebuild the magic circle. To create spaces where people can re-learn the fundamental practice of agreeing on rules. Your junk playground is not a children's activity. It is a prototype for post-war society.

SØRENSEN: There is an English woman — Lady Allen of Hurtwood. She is a landscape architect like me. She has heard about Emdrup and wants to visit. I believe she wants to bring the idea to England.

HUIZINGA: England will need it. When the bombs stop falling, the cities will be full of rubble. And children will play in that rubble, as children always do. The question is whether the adults will see that play as a nuisance to be suppressed, or as the seed of a new civic order.

SØRENSEN: Lady Allen, I think, will see the seed. She is already arguing that bomb sites should be given to children.

HUIZINGA: There is poetry in that. The destroyed city becomes the playground. The instruments of annihilation become the materials of creation. The children rebuild civilization from the ruins — not as their parents designed it, but as they imagine it. In junk. In scrap. In skrammologi.

SØRENSEN: Professor, I have a question that may seem naïve. Can one person build such a playground for themselves?

HUIZINGA: What do you mean?

SØRENSEN: I mean — must this always be a collective act? Must there always be other children? Or can a single individual create their own magic circle?

HUIZINGA: (thinking for a long time) The hermit monks did it. The desert fathers. They drew a circle in the sand and said: "Within this space, I live by my own rules." The artist does it — the studio is a magic circle. The writer does it with a blank page. So yes, I believe it is possible for one person to create their own toverking. But it requires a rare combination: the imagination to conceive of different rules, the courage to enact them, and the discipline to maintain the circle's boundary.

SØRENSEN: You have just described what I do when I design a garden.

HUIZINGA: And what the children do when they build a shack from pallets. The scale is different. The act is the same. An individual claiming a space and filling it with their own order. This is — if I may be so bold — the smallest possible unit of sovereignty.

SØRENSEN: One person. One boundary. One set of self-chosen rules.

HUIZINGA: One person state.

The café is closing. The waiter, an old man who has been trying not to listen, brings the bill. Outside, the streetlights are dimmed for the blackout. Huizinga wraps his scarf tightly — he feels the cold more acutely now. Sørensen holds the door.

SØRENSEN: Will you come see the playground tomorrow? The children will be there.

HUIZINGA: I would like nothing more. But I must be on the night boat. My hosts are nervous.

SØRENSEN: Then I will tell you what Bertelsen wrote in his diary last week. He watched a boy — nine years old, small for his age — spend three days building a shelter from old doors and canvas. On the third day, the boy sat inside his creation and said to no one in particular: "This is my country."

HUIZINGA: (stopping on the threshold) That boy understands something that most political theorists never will.

SØRENSEN: What?

HUIZINGA: That sovereignty is not granted. It is played into existence.

Notes

Historical Context

Johan Huizinga (1872–1945) — Dutch cultural historian, professor at Leiden University. Published Homo Ludens: A Study of the Play-Element in Culture in 1938, arguing that play is not a by-product of culture but its generative force. He demonstrated that law, war, poetry, philosophy, and art all originate in play-forms. His concept of the "magic circle" — a temporary, bounded space where special rules apply — has become foundational in game studies, ritual theory, and increasingly in political philosophy. Huizinga was placed under house arrest by the Nazis in 1942 and died in February 1945, months before liberation.

Carl Theodor Sørensen (1893–1979) — Danish landscape architect, widely regarded as one of the most influential landscape designers of the 20th century. In his book Parkpolitik i Sogn og Købstad (1931), he first proposed the concept of "junk playgrounds" (skrammellegepladser) after observing that children preferred construction sites and junkyards to the formal playgrounds he designed. In 1943, his vision was realized at the Emdrup workers' housing cooperative in Copenhagen, where the world's first planned adventure playground opened during the German occupation of Denmark. The playground was deliberately created as a safe space for children's free play at a time when their street activities might be mistaken for acts of sabotage by occupation forces.

John Bertelsen (1917–1978) — The first pædagog (play worker) at Emdrup. He coined the term skrammologi (junkology) and kept detailed diaries of children's play behavior. Bertelsen stressed that play should be entirely self-directed, with adults intervening only to prevent serious harm. His observations documented how children spontaneously developed governance systems, territorial agreements, and conflict resolution mechanisms.

Key Concepts Referenced

The Magic Circle (Huizinga, 1938): A temporary world within the ordinary world, demarcated by clear boundaries, within which special rules operate. Players enter voluntarily and accept these rules for the duration of play. The concept has been applied to game design (by Katie Salen and Eric Zimmerman), ritual studies, and governance theory. In this dialogue, it is reframed as the primordial form of jurisdiction.

Piaget's Moral Development Theory (1932): Jean Piaget's observation that children progress from "heteronomous morality" (rules are fixed, external, immutable) to "autonomous morality" (rules are social constructs that can be negotiated through mutual consent). The critical transition occurs during collaborative play, particularly games where children modify rules through collective agreement.

Adventure Playgrounds / Junk Playgrounds: A movement that spread from Denmark to the UK (via Lady Marjory Allen of Hurtwood's 1946 Picture Post article "Why Not Use Our Bomb Sites Like This?"), then to Japan, Germany, and beyond. Key sites include Emdrup (Copenhagen, 1943), Clydesdale Road (London, 1952), and The Land (Wrexham, Wales, 2012—present). The philosophy: provide children with raw materials and unstructured space; let them build, destroy, and rebuild their own environment. Trained play workers observe but do not direct.

Aldo van Eyck's Amsterdam Playgrounds (1947–1978): Dutch architect Aldo van Eyck designed over 700 playgrounds in post-war Amsterdam — minimalist geometric forms (sandpits, stepping stones, steel arches) placed in bomb sites, derelict lots, and vacant squares. Van Eyck saw the playground as "the inbetween realm" — neither architecture nor landscape, neither public nor private, but a third space where the child and the city negotiate. His playgrounds were a critique of modernist urban planning (CIAM) and its tendency to treat the city as a machine for adults. Van Eyck's colleague Liane Lefaivre wrote: "Even if van Eyck had designed nothing but this galaxy of playgrounds... his place among the major figures of architecture of this century would have been secured."

The Junior Republic Movement (1895–1940s): Founded by William R. George in Freeville, New York, as a "miniature United States" run entirely by young people aged 5–21. Citizens elected officials, enacted laws, paid taxes in their own currency, staffed courts and jails, published newspapers, and operated stores. By 1908, the National Association of Junior Republics aimed to establish one in every state. Alumni include actor Steve McQueen, Pulitzer Prize-winning journalists William Dapping and Theodore White, and US Surgeon General Julius Richmond. The movement demonstrated that children, given the infrastructure and permission, could operate functional governance systems.

Summerhill School (est. 1921): Founded by A.S. Neill in Suffolk, England, as a democratic free school. All decisions — from curriculum to community rules to disciplinary matters — are made in weekly General School Meetings where every person (student and staff) has one equal vote. Children are not required to attend classes. Neill's philosophy: "I'd rather Summerhill produced a happy street sweeper than a neurotic prime minister." The school has operated continuously for over 100 years.

Freetown Christiania (est. 1971): A self-proclaimed autonomous commune in Copenhagen, founded by squatters who occupied a derelict military base. Governed by consensus in community meetings, with no formal hierarchy. Christiania has its own laws, flag, and currency, and has been in continuous negotiation with the Danish state for over 50 years. It is, in many ways, the adult version of the adventure playground: a reclaimed derelict space, governed by its inhabitants, in a state of perpetual construction and reconstruction.

The Line from Playground to Network State

The intellectual genealogy runs like this:

  1. Huizinga (1938): Culture originates in play. The "magic circle" is the primordial form of jurisdiction — a bounded space with voluntarily accepted rules.
  1. Sørensen & Bertelsen (1943): Children, given materials and freedom, spontaneously create governance structures. The junk playground demonstrates that sovereignty can emerge from the bottom up, without blueprints.
  1. Piaget (1932): The transition from rule-follower to rule-maker (from heteronomous to autonomous morality) happens through collaborative play. The playground is where humans learn to become legislators.
  1. Lady Allen / Adventure Playground Movement (1946–): Bomb sites — spaces of destruction — become spaces of creation. The movement proves that governance structures can be rebuilt from rubble, and that the rebuilders don't need credentials, just materials and permission.
  1. Van Eyck (1947–1978): Architecture should serve the "inbetween realm" — the space where the individual and the collective negotiate. The playground is not an addition to the city; it is the city's essential unit.
  1. Neill / Summerhill (1921–): Democratic self-governance works at scale (60–80 people) across generations, with minimal formal structure. One person, one vote. No coercion.
  1. Christiania (1971–): The adult adventure playground. An experiment in voluntary community on reclaimed territory, proving that self-governance can persist for decades in tension with a nation-state.
  1. Srinivasan / Network State (2022): An online community assembles, agrees on shared values, crowdfunds territory, and seeks diplomatic recognition. The magic circle goes digital — and then claims physical space.
  1. One Person State (Svitla, 2026): The smallest possible magic circle. One person draws a boundary, defines their own rules, builds their infrastructure from whatever materials are available. Not a withdrawal from the world, but the foundational act of play that generates new culture.

Как это вписывается в One Person State

Глава "The Junk Republic" может стать одним из центральных философских кусков книги. Ключевой инсайт: суверенность — это не юридический статус. Это акт игры. Каждое государство началось с того, что кто-то провёл черту и сказал "здесь — другие правила." Huizinga доказал, что культура рождается из игры, а не наоборот. Sørensen доказал, что дети, получив пространство и материалы, спонтанно создают всё то же, что создают государства: территории, законы, торговлю, архитектуру. Значит, One Person State — это не утопия и не юридический хак. Это возвращение к базовому человеческому акту: очертить круг, войти в него, и начать играть по своим правилам.
