First Reader Reaction: "One Person State" by Ray Svitla

Reader profile: Daria, 34, product designer. Kharkiv → Berlin (2022). Tech job at a mid-size SaaS. Cat named Pixel. Reads on planes and in cafes. Subscribed to too many newsletters, finishes maybe 40% of them.

1. Would I pick this up?

Yes — but it depends on where I find it.

The title "One Person State" — I'd stop scrolling. Not because I know what it means but because it sounds like something I've been trying to say in therapy for two years. "State" as in country or "state" as in condition? Both? That ambiguity would hook me.

If I see it in a bookstore (Do The Right Thing in Berlin, or that English shelf at Ocelot), I'd pick it up. If someone shared it on LinkedIn with "great read on personal branding 🚀" I'd ignore it forever.

The back-cover pitch matters enormously. "Before you can build a network state, you need to be able to run yourself" — that's good. That's a sentence I'd read twice. But if the back cover leads with Balaji or crypto-state language, I'm putting it down. If it leads with the immigrant angle and the "shipped everything, chose nothing" energy — I'm buying it.

The author being Belarusian-born and doing Minsk→Kyiv→Lisbon — that's not abstract for me. That's someone I might know. That alone gets it past my filter.

2. Chapter-by-chapter gut reaction

1. the node — "you can't build a network state if you can't run yourself"

Yes, tell me more.

This is the promise. This is why I'd buy the book. I don't want to build a network state. I want to run myself. If this chapter is personal and honest — like, here's what it feels like to not be able to run yourself, here's what it looks like when your infrastructure is someone else's visa policy — I'm in. If it's a manifesto about decentralization with a capital D, I'm already checking my phone.

2. three resets — Minsk→Kyiv→Lisbon, autobiography as methodology

Yes, tell me more.

This is the chapter I'd read first. Honestly, I might flip to this one in the store. Minsk→Kyiv→Lisbon is a specific kind of story I've never seen in a book that wasn't a memoir about suffering. "Autobiography as methodology" — I like that framing. It says: my life isn't just a story, it's data. That's how I think about my own move. Not "I fled a war" (even though I did) but "I ran an experiment and here's what I learned." I want this chapter to be uncomfortable and funny and not ask for sympathy.

3. the dead priest — Ivan Illich, philosopher who predicted what AI tools cost us

Maybe.

I don't know who Ivan Illich is. The title "the dead priest" is intriguing — dark, weird, I'd give it five pages. But "philosopher who predicted what AI tools cost us" feels like it could go either way. If it's "here's a guy from the 70s who basically described your relationship with Figma and ChatGPT" — yes. If it's 30 pages of intellectual history and deschooling theory — I'm skimming. The risk here is that Ray loves this guy and gives him too much room. I need to feel why this matters to ME, not why it matters to Ray.

4. proof of sovereignty — what your "configuration" means in practice

Yes, tell me more.

"Configuration" — that's a dev word being used for a human thing, and it works. I configure Figma. I configure my apartment. I configure my life in Berlin. But no one's ever asked me to look at my own configuration file. What are my defaults? What did I inherit from Kharkiv? What did I install in Berlin? If this chapter is a practical mirror — something I can actually use — it might be the best chapter in the book.

5. phantom states — 5 historical models of governance without territory

I'd skip this.

Sorry. I can tell this is well-researched. Jewish parliament 1580, Malta, Tibet, Sealand — these are cool Wikipedia articles. But this is where the book stops being about me and starts being about the author's research interests. I'd flip through it. If each example is two pages and directly connected to "here's what this means for you, a person with no fixed country," maybe. But if it's five mini-essays about historical governance models, I'm jumping to chapter 6. This feels like the chapter that's in the book because the author finds it fascinating, not because the reader needs it.

6. the decision layer — what's left when AI makes output cheap

Yes, tell me more.

This is the question I think about at work every single day. I design interfaces. AI can now design interfaces. What am I? What's left? If this chapter has an actual answer — not "humans are creative" (vomit) but something real about what decisions are and why they're different from output — I'd underline things. I'd photograph pages. This could be the chapter I text someone about.

7. context gap — 10 loops where humans and machines diverge

Maybe.

"10 loops" sounds like a listicle. It also sounds like it could be incredible if each loop is a small, specific, recognizable moment. Like: "you know when you redesign something and the AI version is technically better but something is off and you can't explain what?" If it's that — yes. If it's an abstract taxonomy of human-machine differences — I've read that article already, multiple times.

8. vernacular domain — building outside institutional permission

Yes, tell me more.

I don't know what "vernacular domain" means but "building outside institutional permission" — that's my entire life in Berlin. My German isn't good enough to navigate bureaucracy alone. My Ukrainian degree means nothing here. Everything I've built — my career, my freelance clients, my sense of home — I built outside the system, not inside it. If this chapter names that, I'll feel something.

9. swarm of one — biological models: how insects govern without leaders

Lost me.

I can see what this is doing intellectually. Decentralized governance, emergent behavior, ant colonies, etc. But this is where I'd feel like I'm reading a TED talk. "What if I told you... bees have already solved this problem?" I don't want to read about bees. I want to read about me. Unless this is extremely short and used as a poetic metaphor rather than a scientific argument, it breaks the spell of the personal voice.

10. body as first territory — somatic sovereignty, dance/body as infrastructure

Maybe — leaning yes.

This is unexpected in a book like this, and that's why it's interesting. Most "sovereignty" books live entirely in the head. Body as territory — I actually feel that. My body carried me out of Ukraine. My body sits in a Herman Miller chair in Berlin designing someone else's product. But "somatic sovereignty" and "dance as infrastructure" could easily tip into woo-woo territory. If it stays grounded in the immigrant body — the body that crosses borders, that gets checked at airports, that holds tension from a language it can't speak yet — I'm here for it. If it becomes a chapter about ecstatic dance workshops in Lisbon, I'm closing the book.

11. the monastery form — what you write in the "country" field on forms

Yes, tell me more.

"What you write in the 'country' field on forms." I felt that in my chest. Every single time. Every dropdown menu. Every time I have to pick between "Ukraine" (where I'm from but can't go back to), "Germany" (where I live but don't belong), and the absence of an option that means "it's complicated." If this chapter is the closing chapter, it's the right one. It takes something bureaucratic and tiny and shows that it's actually the whole question of the book. This is the chapter that would make me cry on a plane.

3. Where do I feel seen?

  • "shipped everything, chose nothing" — I moved to Berlin and optimized everything. Apartment, job, gym, routine. And I still feel like I'm performing a life rather than living one. This sentence is a knife.
  • "portable not because it's light. because it's mine." — This is exactly the distinction no one makes. Everyone talks about being "location independent" like it's freedom. It's not freedom. It's just... mine. My mess, my system, my cat, my three different messaging apps for three different countries.
  • The "country" field on forms — see above. This is the most universally specific moment in the whole concept.
  • "I'm not a person, I'm a pipeline" — I felt this last Tuesday at 2am when I was automating my own Notion workflow instead of sleeping. I am optimizing myself into a product. Someone naming that is valuable.
  • Three resets as methodology — The idea that my displacement isn't just a sad story but a method, a way of learning — that reframes something I carry as damage into something I carry as knowledge. That's powerful. That's the thing I've been trying to articulate.

4. Where do I get bored?

  • Phantom states (ch. 5) — Historical governance models. My eyes are already glazing. Unless each one is a single page with a brutal "here's why this matters to you" pivot, it reads like someone's thesis chapter that survived the edit.
  • Swarm of one (ch. 9) — Ant colony metaphors for human governance. I've seen this talk. I've seen it at re:publica. I've seen it in a viral Twitter thread. It's not that it's wrong — it's that it feels like borrowed credibility from biology to make a social point seem more scientific.
  • Context gap (ch. 7) — if it's a list. Ten loops. If each loop is a small essay with a real anecdote, fine. If it's "Loop 1: Pattern Recognition. Loop 2: Contextual Awareness. Loop 3:..." — that's a blog post, not a book chapter.
  • Any time the book sounds like it's teaching me. The quotes that work ("shipped everything, chose nothing") are confessions. The moments that risk losing me are when the voice shifts from "here's what happened to me" to "here's what you should understand about decentralization." The second voice is every Medium article I've never finished.

5. The friend text

ok so I found this book by a belarusian guy who moved minsk→kyiv→lisbon and it's about like... being your own country? not in a crypto way. more like — you know that feeling when you fill out a form and there's no right answer for "country"? it's about THAT. and about what's left of you when AI does your job. some chapters are uneven but there's this one line — "shipped everything, chose nothing" — and I just sat there for like five minutes. you should read it. or at least the first three chapters

6. What's missing from MY perspective

  • The bureaucracy chapter. I want a chapter about Ausländerbehörde. About the visa appointment at 6am. About the Anmeldung. About the specific violence of European bureaucracy toward Eastern Europeans who are "the right kind of immigrant" but still not European enough. The book talks about sovereignty as a concept — but the daily experience of NOT having it is standing in a government office with a number ticket, hoping someone speaks English.
  • The money question. Sovereignty costs money. What does "running yourself" look like when you're sending money home? When your savings are in a currency that collapsed? When your salary in Berlin is good by Kharkiv standards and mediocre by Berlin standards? The class dimension of sovereignty is missing.
  • Community vs. sovereignty. The whole book seems to be about the individual — the "one person" state. But my actual experience of displacement is that I survive because of group chats, Ukrainian cafes in Berlin, friends who moved here the same month I did. Where are the other people? Is sovereignty lonely? Does it have to be?
  • The guilt. I left. My parents didn't. My friends who stayed are living a different life. Every time I "optimize my sovereignty" I feel the distance from people who can't. The book might address this in chapter 2 — but if it doesn't, it feels incomplete. Sovereignty isn't just a project. It's a privilege.
  • Dating / relationships across borders. This sounds trivial but it's not. What does sovereignty mean when your partner is from a different country, different language, different set of forms? When "where do we live" is a negotiation between visa regimes?

7. The bookshelf question

This goes next to:

  • Trick Mirror by Jia Tolentino (essays about performing yourself)
  • Refuse to Choose by Barbara Sher (the multipotentialite thing, guilty pleasure)
  • Sapiens but cooler and more personal
  • Not next to Balaji. Not next to the tech books. This is the "identity" shelf, not the "future" shelf.

Would I display it? Yes — if the cover is good. If it's minimal, lowercase, maybe with that 🐌. If it looks like an O'Reilly book or a startup manual, it goes in the drawer.

The spine test: "ONE PERSON STATE" on a spine — that's a conversation starter. Someone sees it and asks "what's that?" and I get to explain it, and explaining it feels like explaining myself.

8. Honest purchase decision

Would I pay €20? Yes. Not for every chapter — but the promise of chapters 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 11 is worth €20. I've paid more for worse.

Would I read it on a plane? Absolutely. This is a perfect Kyiv→Berlin or Berlin→Lisbon book. (I'd probably buy it specifically for a flight.)

Would I finish it? Honestly — 70% chance. I'd read chapters 1-4 straight through. I'd skip or skim 5 and 9. I'd come back for 6, 8, and 11. Chapter 7 depends on execution. Chapter 10 depends on whether it stays grounded. The non-linear structure actually helps — I can skip without feeling lost.

Would I recommend it? To specific people — yes. To my Ukrainian friends in Berlin — definitely. To my German colleagues — maybe, if they've ever asked me "but where are you FROM from?" To my parents — no, they'd find it self-indulgent. To my therapist — honestly, maybe.

The real test: Would I quote it in a conversation? Would I say "there's this book that talks about..." when I'm trying to explain something about my life? Based on the quotes I've seen — yes. "Shipped everything, chose nothing" and "portable not because it's light, because it's mine" are already living in my head.

That's the highest compliment I can give a book: it gave me language for something I was already feeling.

Written as Daria, 34, Berlin. Pixel says hi. 🐈
