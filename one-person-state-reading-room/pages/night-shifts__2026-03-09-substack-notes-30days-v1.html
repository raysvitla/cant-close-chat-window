DAY 1 — March 9, Sunday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 1 + LinkedIn Day 1

NOTE 1:

the country I needed didn't exist. so I'm building it.

I'm Belarusian. I live in Lisbon. I lost a 7-figure net worth not to a crash or a scam but to context switching — running 7 projects simultaneously and making progress on none. I have a Maine Coon named Heidi who has veto power over all decisions made after 10pm.

this newsletter is called 404Embassy. the name is a joke that isn't a joke. the country returns a 404 error. we're writing the code.

for the next 30 days I'm posting daily on three platforms — Threads, LinkedIn, and here. same ideas, different languages. Substack gets the real version.

NOTE 2:

I'm also posting daily on Threads and LinkedIn for 30 days. the Threads version is shorter and dumber. the LinkedIn version wears a tie. this is the version that gets to think out loud.

consider this the director's commentary.

DAY 2 — March 10, Monday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 2

NOTE:

you've switched jobs. switched cities. switched banks, partners, diets, phone carriers.

but you've never switched governments.

the one system that controls the most about your life is the one you have zero ability to change. we update our phones every year and our governance systems roughly never.

the Substack crowd probably already knows about Balaji's "The Network State." I've read it three times. I agree with about 60% of it. the other 40% is where 404Embassy lives — in the gap between the theory and what actual displaced people need.

what's the one thing about governance you think everyone gets wrong?

DAY 3 — March 11, Tuesday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 3

NOTE:

I tracked my screen time manually for 16 days. not with an app — with a notebook and a pen, like some kind of cave person.

the finding that broke me: I mass-produce output until 2pm. emails, decks, documents. pure execution. then I crash. not from tiredness but from having spent 6 hours doing things without once deciding if they were the right things.

every AI tool I use is optimized for DO. none of them help me BE. we're building a civilization optimized for throughput and I'm not sure that's the same as progress.

DAY 6 — March 14, Friday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 6

NOTE:

when I left Belarus, the border guard looked at my passport for a very long time. then at me. then at the passport again.

I remember thinking: this man I've never met is deciding whether my life continues or pauses.

one stranger. one stamp. that's the entire system.

we've built mass transit, gene editing, and AI that writes poetry — but crossing an imaginary line still depends on a stranger's mood at 6am.

I keep coming back to this moment whenever I write about sovereignty. it's not an abstract concept when you've felt the weight of it in a passport control booth at dawn.

DAY 8 — March 16, Sunday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 7

NOTE:

you signed a Terms of Service you never read when you were born. it's called the social contract.

it dictates where you can work. how much of your income you keep. what you can put in your body. who you can marry in some places.

you didn't agree to it. you can't fork it. there's no competitor offering better terms.

Rousseau wrote about this in 1762. 264 years later and we still haven't shipped v2. if that's not institutional tech debt, I don't know what is.

the question 404Embassy keeps circling: how do you refactor a system you can't take offline?

DAY 9 — March 17, Monday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 8

NOTE:

my wife sent me a Psychology Today article titled "is your screen time destroying your relationship?"

I read it. then I built a tracking system to measure my screen time more precisely. she was not impressed.

but the data showed something unexpected. I wasn't spending too much time on screens. I was spending time on screens at the wrong times. 7am: phone before coffee. before eye contact. 8pm: laptop during the only 2 hours we're both home and awake.

I didn't reduce my screen time. I relocated it. same output. completely different marriage.

sometimes the problem isn't quantity. it's placement.

DAY 14 — March 22, Saturday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 14

NOTE:

I once filled out a registration form for a monastery in Georgia.

not because I was becoming a monk. because I was researching how small communities manage membership.

the form asked: name. purpose of visit. skills you bring. what you hope to learn. how long you intend to stay. what you'll contribute.

six questions.

then I looked at a citizenship application for a European country. 47 documents. 6 appointments. proof of income for 5 years.

the monastery wanted to know who you are. the country wanted to know if you qualify.

there's a difference between a community that invites and an institution that filters. I think about that monastery form every time I work on 404Embassy's membership model.

DAY 15 — March 23, Sunday

Source: original — Substack-native

NOTE:

a question for newsletter writers who cover governance, sovereignty, policy, or institutional design:

how do you make people care about infrastructure?

I keep running into this wall. the actual work of building alternative governance is boring. identity systems. conflict resolution frameworks. decision-making protocols. it's plumbing.

nobody writes manifestos about plumbing. but plumbing is what makes a city work.

the sexy version of this work is "build a new country." the real version is "design a dispute resolution process that works across time zones."

how do you write about the plumbing without losing people?

DAY 17 — March 25, Tuesday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 22

NOTE:

when you hate how things work, you have two options. leave, or stay and fight to change it.

Albert Hirschman called these "exit" and "voice" in 1970.

I'm an exit person. always have been. bad job? quit. bad city? move. bad system? build a new one. 404Embassy is the most "exit" project possible.

but 17 days of writing in public taught me something: you can't build an alternative without voice. without explaining, persuading, arguing, listening. pure exit is just running away with a business plan.

if you haven't read "Exit, Voice, and Loyalty" — it's 55 years old and still the best framework for understanding why people leave organizations or countries.

are you more exit or voice?

DAY 19 — March 27, Thursday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 25

NOTE:

Ivan Illich argued in 1973 that tools should serve people, not the other way around. he called them "convivial tools" — tools that expand what you can do without creating dependence.

a bicycle is convivial. it makes you faster without making you dependent.

now look at AI through this lens. ChatGPT makes you faster. but it requires internet, a subscription, a company's server, that company's policy decisions, and trust that they won't change terms tomorrow.

the convivial version of AI would run on your machine. owned by you. no subscription. no terms of service.

we're building the car version of AI and calling it liberation. Illich would have had opinions.

"Tools for Conviviality" — 1973, free PDF online. reads like it was written about the AI debate.

DAY 21 — March 29, Saturday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 15

NOTE:

hot take: your HOA is a government.

it collects taxes (fees). it makes laws (bylaws). it enforces them (fines). it has elections that 4 people show up to. it controls your property.

the difference between an HOA and a country is scale and weapons.

this isn't a joke — it's actually the most honest way to understand how governance works. small groups making rules that affect your daily life, mostly run by whoever bothers to show up.

this is also why I think governance innovation will come from small communities, not nation-states. the HOA scale is where you can actually experiment. the nation-state scale is where experiments go to die in committee.

DAY 22 — March 30, Sunday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 23

NOTE:

there are roughly 1.5 million Belarusians living outside Belarus.

we have no shared infrastructure. no coordination. no way to make collective decisions. we have WhatsApp groups. Telegram channels. Facebook pages run by volunteers.

imagine a company of 1.5 million people with no org chart, no communication system, no decision-making process, and no budget. that's a diaspora.

every displaced community faces this. Ukrainians, Syrians, Venezuelans, Hongkongers. millions of people who share identity, values, and goals — with zero infrastructure to act on any of it.

this is the problem 404Embassy exists to solve. not with ideology. with plumbing.

if you're part of a diaspora — what infrastructure do you wish existed?

DAY 23 — March 31, Monday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 22-24

NOTE 1:

the tools to build a country already exist.

Estonia's e-Residency lets you run an EU company without living in Estonia. that's a government selling access to its infrastructure — like a SaaS product.

DAOs have managed billions of dollars with voting systems that would make most parliaments jealous. Gitcoin has funded $50M+ in public goods through quadratic funding.

the missing piece isn't technology. it's community. a group of people who say "we want to live under rules we actually chose." that's what 404Embassy is trying to be.

DAY 26 — April 3, Thursday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 19

NOTE:

voting is the minimum viable participation in a society. and we treat it like the maximum.

"I voted" is the end of civic engagement for most people. badge earned. back to normal for 4 years.

but think about how you participate in a company you care about. you don't just vote on the board once a year and go home. you show up daily. you give feedback. you propose changes.

Wikipedia runs on ongoing participation. open source runs on it. every functional online community runs on it. we've solved this problem everywhere except the one place it matters most.

404Embassy is designing layered participation models — voting as floor, not ceiling. I wrote about this in the newsletter.

DAY 27 — April 4, Friday

Source: original — Substack-native

NOTE:

a question I keep getting asked and don't have a good answer to:

"how is 404Embassy different from every other network state project?"

most network state projects start with ideology. libertarian paradise. crypto utopia. seasteading fantasy.

404Embassy starts with a specific population — the Belarusian diaspora — and a specific problem — no coordination infrastructure. it's not "what if we built a perfect society?" it's "1.5 million people need to make decisions together and currently can't."

theory is easy. plumbing for real people is hard. I chose the plumbing.

is that different enough? genuinely asking.

DAY 28 — April 5, Saturday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 28

NOTE:

my AI agent can research faster than any human I've worked with. it cannot tell when I'm asking the wrong question.

I spent a week having an agent analyze governance models. beautiful, thorough, useless work. useless because the real question wasn't "which governance model works best" but "why do I keep avoiding asking the community what they want."

the agent answered what I asked. a human collaborator would have said "Ray, you're procrastinating with research again."

emotional intelligence isn't detecting feelings. it's knowing when someone is hiding from themselves. no algorithm does that.

DAY 29 — April 6, Sunday

Source: original — meta/30-day reflection

NOTE 1:

29 days of posting daily on three platforms. here's the honest accounting.

the posts about being lost outperformed the posts about having answers. on every platform. every single time.

Substack Notes had the best conversations. Threads had the most reach. LinkedIn had the most "let's get on a call" energy (not always a compliment).

the ideas that worked everywhere: passport lottery, the social contract as ToS, the monastery membership form, Heidi's pet passport. these are the ideas that survived compression, corporatization, and scrutiny. they're real.

NOTE 2:

the most surprising thing about posting daily for 30 days: I didn't get better at writing. I got better at noticing what I actually think versus what I think I should think.

turns out those are very different things and the gap between them is where all the interesting writing lives.
