DAY 4 — March 12, Wednesday

Source: original — governance glitch of the week #1

NOTE:

governance glitch of the week: Estonia's e-Residency program lets you register an EU company without ever setting foot in Estonia. 100,000+ people have done it. it's a government selling access to its infrastructure like a SaaS product.

the glitch: you get a business identity but not a political one. you can invoice through Estonia but you can't vote there. you pay for governance but have zero voice in it. taxation without representation, voluntarily entered into, in 2026.

DAY 6 — March 14, Friday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 6 (expanded with Substack-only context)

NOTE:

when I left Belarus, the border guard looked at my passport for a very long time. then at me. then at the passport again.

I remember thinking: this man I've never met is deciding whether my life continues or pauses.

one stranger. one stamp. that's the entire system.

we've built mass transit, gene editing, and AI that writes poetry — but crossing an imaginary line still depends on a stranger's mood at 6am.

what I haven't written about publicly before: I had a backup plan that morning. a second route through a different crossing, papers for a story that wasn't true, and a phone number I was supposed to call if the first stamp didn't come. I keep that phone number saved. I don't know why. the person on the other end probably changed their number years ago.

DAY 7 — March 15, Saturday

Source: book recommendation — Hirschman

Newsletter link: n/a

NOTE:

book that rewired how I think about countries: Albert Hirschman, "Exit, Voice, and Loyalty" (1970).

when you hate how things work, you have two options. leave (exit) or stay and fight to change it (voice). Hirschman's insight: the threat of exit is what gives voice its power. a government that knows you can't leave has no reason to listen.

this is why authoritarian states close borders. not to keep enemies out — to keep citizens in. once exit is impossible, voice becomes decoration.

55 years old. 130 pages. still the best framework for understanding why people leave organizations, companies, or countries. free PDF exists if you look.

DAY 8 — March 16, Sunday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 7 (tighter, no question ending)

NOTE:

you signed a Terms of Service you never read when you were born. it's called the social contract.

it dictates where you can work. how much of your income you keep. what you can put in your body. who you can marry in some places.

you didn't agree to it. you can't fork it. there's no competitor offering better terms.

Rousseau wrote about this in 1762. 264 years later and we still haven't shipped v2. if that's not institutional tech debt, I don't know what is.

DAY 11 — March 19, Wednesday

Source: original — governance glitch of the week #2

NOTE:

governance glitch of the week: in 2024, a DAO called Nouns governed a treasury of $50M+ using on-chain voting. any token holder could propose how to spend funds. proposals were public. votes were transparent. execution was automatic.

then someone figured out you could buy enough tokens to pass a proposal that drained the treasury into your own wallet. which is exactly what happened.

the glitch: transparent governance without exit costs is just a hostile takeover waiting to happen. Hirschman predicted this — when exit is too cheap, loyalty never forms.

most "governance innovation" fails because it solves for transparency but not for commitment.

DAY 17 — March 25, Tuesday

Source: book recommendation — James C. Scott

Newsletter link: n/a

NOTE:

book that should be required reading for anyone building institutions: James C. Scott, "Seeing Like a State" (1998).

Scott's argument: states simplify complex realities to make them legible — standardized names, grid cities, monoculture forests. the simplification makes governance possible but destroys the local knowledge that made things work.

every centralized system does this. every government form that asks you to pick one nationality. every database that requires a permanent address. the form can't hold the reality, so the reality gets trimmed.

404Embassy's design problem in one sentence: how do you build infrastructure without flattening the people it serves.

DAY 18 — March 26, Wednesday

Source: original — governance glitch of the week #3

NOTE:

governance glitch of the week: Switzerland has 2,136 municipalities. some have fewer than 50 residents. they still hold town meetings where every citizen votes directly on budgets, building permits, and local laws.

a Swiss friend told me his village of 200 people voted on whether to repaint the community hall. turnout was 73%. the debate lasted two hours.

meanwhile, US congressional elections average 40% turnout for decisions affecting 750,000 people.

the glitch isn't that direct democracy doesn't scale. it's that representative democracy doesn't motivate.

DAY 19 — March 27, Thursday

Source: adapted from LinkedIn Day 25 (book rec — Illich, standalone)

NOTE:

Ivan Illich, "Tools for Conviviality" (1973). free PDF online. reads like it was written last week about the AI debate.

Illich's argument: tools should serve people without creating dependence. a bicycle is convivial — it makes you faster without requiring a subscription, a company's server, or trust that someone won't change the terms tomorrow.

now look at ChatGPT through this lens. it makes you faster. but it requires internet, a subscription, a corporation's policy decisions, and faith in their continued existence.

we're building the car version of AI and calling it liberation. the convivial version would run on your machine, owned by you.

Illich would have had opinions.

DAY 21 — March 29, Saturday

Source: adapted from Threads Day 15 (HOA — expanded with actual argument)

NOTE:

your HOA is a government.

it collects taxes (fees). it makes laws (bylaws). it enforces them (fines). it has elections that 4 people show up to.

this isn't a joke — it's the most honest way to understand governance. small groups making rules that affect your daily life, mostly run by whoever bothers to show up.

governance innovation will come from small communities, not nation-states. the HOA scale is where you can experiment. the nation-state scale is where experiments go to die in committee. every successful governance model in history started small — Athenian democracy was 30,000 citizens. the Swiss cantons started as mutual defense pacts between villages.

Newsletter link: embassy.svit.la/p/participation

NOTE:

governance glitch of the week: voting is the minimum viable participation in a society. and we treat it like the maximum.

"I voted" is the end of civic engagement for most people. badge earned. back to normal for 4 years.

but think about how you participate in anything you actually care about. Wikipedia runs on daily participation. open source runs on it. you don't vote on the Linux kernel once a year and go home.

404Embassy is designing layered participation — voting as floor, not ceiling. different levels of involvement, different levels of influence. the full model is in the newsletter → embassy.svit.la/p/participation

DAY 27 — April 4, Friday

Newsletter link: embassy.svit.la/p/e-residency

NOTE:

practical thing: how Estonia's e-Residency actually works if you're considering it.

cost: 100 EUR + biometric appointment at an Estonian embassy. processing: 3-8 weeks. what you get: an EU digital identity, ability to register and run an Estonian company, access to EU banking.

what you don't get: residency. visa. voting rights. healthcare. any physical presence rights whatsoever.

it's useful for a specific thing (EU company formation without EU residency) and useless for everything else. I wrote the full honest breakdown including the three scenarios where it makes sense and the five where it doesn't → embassy.svit.la/p/e-residency

DAY 29 — April 6, Sunday

Source: original — what I'm reading this week #3 + book rec (Scott)

NOTE:

what I'm reading this week:

rereading James C. Scott's "Against the Grain" — his argument that early states were basically protection rackets that forced nomads into grain farming because grain is easy to tax. not because grain was better. because grain was legible.

states don't optimize for human flourishing. they optimize for legibility. this is the single most useful lens for understanding why governments do what they do.
