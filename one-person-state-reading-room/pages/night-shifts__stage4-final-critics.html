Line editor

Going sentence by sentence:

  1. "Last autumn I filled out a registration form for an Ivan Illich centenary gathering at a monastery in Tuscany." — Clean. Sets scene without fat.
  1. "Scholars, theologians, a few activists." — Good rhythm, three beats.
  1. "I wrote 'network state.'" — Lands. The quotes around it do the work.
  1. "Half a joke. I sat with it for ten minutes before I hit submit." — Two short sentences, good tension. Keep.
  1. "The honest answer — 'I genuinely don't know anymore' — wouldn't fit in the field." — "genuinely" is filler. Cut to: "I don't know anymore". Sharper.
  1. "Left Belarus in my twenties after getting too close to how dictatorship works from the inside." — Strong, specific enough without over-explaining.
  1. "War started on a Thursday." — Excellent. Concrete detail that hits.
  1. "the language I dreamed in when solving product problems" — "dreamed in" is more visceral than "thought in." Keep. But "when solving product problems" is clunky — try "when I was debugging."
  1. "I'm not romanticizing this." — Direct. Addresses privilege without being performative.
  1. "The answer was: less than I thought." — Good landing.
  1. "Not rebuilding in another country. Building something that doesn't need a country to validate it." — Uses a parallel structure but it's not the "not X — Y" AI tell. It's two sentences. Fine.
  1. "No model would generate that prompt." — Crisp. Keep.
  1. "Forty years of scholarship on conviviality, and he's writing to a Belarusian newsletter guy in Lisbon." — Great contrast. The self-deprecation works.
  1. "Output is cheap. The decision layer — what you choose to build, what you refuse, what weird corner you stand in — is what's scarce." — The parenthetical list is fine because it specifies rather than ornaments. Keep.
  1. "I could talk to all of them." — Punchline arrives fast after the setup. Good.
  1. "You don't find your actual operating system until the labels come off." — "operating system" is a dev metaphor that fits the voice. Strong line.
  1. "Shipped everything, chose nothing." — Four words. Perfect.
  1. "Illich called the version he cared about the vernacular domain — the space where you do things for yourself, in your own way, outside institutional permission." — The Sajay critic wanted this. It's substantive without being academic.
  1. "Portable not because it's light. Because it's mine." — Two fragments. Works as a closer. Not the "not X — Y" pattern — it's "not because X. Because Y." Different enough.

Cuts:

  • "genuinely" in the opener → cut
  • "when solving product problems" → "when I was debugging"

No other issues found.

Title judge

"country: network state"

  • Colon format mirrors the form field from the opening — readers who finish the piece will get the callback
  • Lowercase per style guide
  • Mysterious enough to click, specific enough to not be clickbait
  • Distinct from previous titles: "fork yourself before the state", "the ritual kaleidoscope", "a dead priest and your AI problem"
  • VERDICT: Strong. Keep.

Subtitle: "three resets and a monastery form"

  • Tells you it's personal (three resets = story)
  • "monastery form" is unusual enough to intrigue
  • Short
  • VERDICT: Good. Keep.

Alternative considered: "what happens after you rebuild your identity three times" — too explanatory, too long. "three resets and a monastery form" is better.

Forward test

Would you forward this to a friend?

Yes. Three reasons:

  1. The Sajay cold-email is a "holy shit, that actually happened" moment — instant screenshot material
  2. The monastery form opening is memorable and unusual — people will retell it
  3. The triple reset gives the piece a backbone that's genuinely personal, not framework-first

Who would you forward it to?

  • A friend in tech who's having an identity crisis about AI
  • A diaspora founder who's rebuilt in a new country
  • Anyone who's ever filled out a "country" field and paused

What might stop someone from forwarding?

  • The piece is personal to Ray. It's not "10 tips." Some people only forward actionable stuff.
  • The Illich references might feel niche if you don't know the previous issues.

Mitigation: The piece works even if you've never heard of Illich. The cold-email section explains enough context. The personal story carries it.

VERDICT: Forwardable. Ship it.
