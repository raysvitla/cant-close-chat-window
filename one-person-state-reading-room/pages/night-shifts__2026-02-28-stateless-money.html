Stateless Money: 7 Parallel Financial Systems Built by Peoples Without States — and What Network States Should Steal From Them

Night research · 28 Feb 2026 · Promen for Ray

Thesis

Every stateless people eventually invents its own money. Not always coins or tokens — sometimes it's a ledger of trust, a rotating pot, a letter that flies faster than gold. The pattern is so consistent across history that it might be a law: sovereignty begins with the ability to move value among your own people without asking permission.

Network States talk endlessly about tokens. But most token designs start from crypto-native first principles and ignore 1,200+ years of real-world experiments in stateless finance. This is a mistake. The diaspora money systems that actually worked share design patterns that Web3 hasn't fully absorbed.

Here are seven systems, what made them work, and what a Belarus Network State (or any network state) should extract from each.

1. Hawala (حِوالة) — The Trust Ledger

Origin: South Asia / Middle East, 8th century CE

Still active: Yes — estimated $200B+ annually flows through hawala networks

How it works: You give money to Broker A in London. Broker A calls Broker B in Mogadishu. Broker B pays out the equivalent. No money physically moves. The brokers settle later through trade, reverse transactions, or cash. The entire system runs on honour between brokers (hawaladars).

Why it survived 1,200 years:

  • Zero infrastructure requirements — works in failed states, war zones, anywhere
  • Cheaper and faster than banks (1-3% vs. 7-10% for formal remittances)
  • The "ledger" is a web of mutual debt between trusted brokers
  • Enforcement is reputational, not legal: cheat once, you're out of the network forever

Design pattern for network states:

Trust as infrastructure. Hawala proves you don't need a blockchain to run a trustless system — you need a high-trust network with catastrophic penalties for defection. Network states obsess over trustless smart contracts but ignore that their communities are already high-trust. A hybrid model — on-chain settlement between strangers, reputation-based settlement between citizens — would be cheaper and faster.

2. Fei-ch'ien (飛錢) — Flying Money

Origin: Tang Dynasty China, ~807 CE

Lasted: ~200 years before state co-optation

How it works: A merchant in Canton deposits copper coins with a local agent and receives a paper certificate. He travels to Chang'an (the capital) with only the paper. There, he presents it to the government office or affiliated merchant and receives cash. The certificate literally "flies" while the heavy coins stay put.

Why it mattered:

  • Solved a real problem: copper coins were heavy, roads were dangerous
  • Started as a merchant innovation, not a state project
  • The Tang government initially banned it (threatened state monopoly on currency), then absorbed and formalized it — which eventually led to the world's first government-issued paper money (jiaozi, Song Dynasty)

Design pattern for network states:

Successful parallel currencies get co-opted, not destroyed. If your network state builds something that works, states will want to regulate it, ban it, or copy it. The fei-ch'ien playbook: build something so useful that the state's best move is absorption, not suppression. Design for eventual diplomatic recognition, not permanent opposition.

3. The Julfan Armenian Trade Network

Origin: New Julfa (Isfahan, Persia), 16th-18th century

Reach: From Manila to Amsterdam to Calcutta

How it works: Armenian silk merchants from the small suburb of New Julfa in Isfahan built one of history's most sophisticated long-distance trade networks. They used a system of commenda contracts (a kind of profit-sharing partnership), maintained correspondence networks across three continents, and developed their own merchant law that operated independently of any state's legal system.

Why it was extraordinary:

  • An ethnic/religious minority (Christian Armenians in a Safavid Muslim empire) with no sovereign state
  • Their network spanned the entire known world — Indian Ocean to Mediterranean
  • They had their own legal code for commercial disputes, enforced by community reputation
  • Shared language + shared religion + diaspora bonds = instant trust infrastructure across 10,000 km

Design pattern for network states:

Diaspora is not a weakness — it's a distributed infrastructure. The Julfan Armenians turned statelessness into a competitive advantage: they had nodes everywhere, spoke a language outsiders couldn't crack, and settled disputes faster than any court. A Belarus Network State with members in Warsaw, Vilnius, Lisbon, Berlin, and Tbilisi already has the topology. It needs the protocol.

4. Susu / Tontine — The Rotating Trust Engine

Origin: West Africa, pre-colonial (exact date unknown)

Still active: Yes — massive in African, Caribbean, and Asian diaspora communities worldwide

Also known as: Esusu, Sou-sou, Hui (Chinese), Chit fund (India), Tandas (Latin America), Paluwagan (Philippines)

How it works: A group of (usually) 10-20 people each contribute a fixed amount weekly or monthly. Each period, one person takes the entire pot. The cycle repeats until everyone has received once. No interest. No bank. No contract. Pure social commitment.

Why it persists globally:

  • Access to lump sums without credit scores, banks, or collateral
  • Enforced by social shame — defaulting means exile from your community
  • Acts as forced savings for people who would otherwise spend incrementally
  • Built and maintained by women in the vast majority of documented cases

The numbers: In many African diaspora communities, ROSCAs (Rotating Savings and Credit Associations) circulate more money than all microfinance institutions combined.

Design pattern for network states:

Micro-solidarity is a monetary technology. DAOs have treasuries, but they don't have susu circles. A network state that implements small-group rotating funds (10-20 citizens, monthly cycle, on-chain or off) creates bonding capital — people who are financially intertwined with each other. This is stronger than token governance. It's closer to citizenship as a felt reality.

5. The Jewish Bills of Exchange (Suftaja / Mamram)

Origin: Medieval Europe and Mediterranean, 10th-15th century

Context: Jews were barred from owning land, restricted to cities, and pushed into financial intermediation

How it works: Jewish merchant families across Europe — and later, the Sephardic networks spanning the Ottoman Empire, Netherlands, and Americas — developed sophisticated bills of exchange that allowed value transfer across borders. A letter from a trusted family in Venice could be cashed by their relatives in Constantinople.

Why it shaped the world:

  • Built the proto-infrastructure of international banking
  • The Rothschild system (18th-19th century) scaled this to nation-state level
  • The network's core technology was family trust across geography — the exact same pattern as hawala, but scaled through intermarriage and religious solidarity
  • When expelled from one country, the network simply rerouted

Design pattern for network states:

Anti-fragility through expulsion. Jewish financial networks got stronger when persecuted because the community became tighter and the diaspora wider. Every expulsion created a new node. Network states should build systems where losing a physical location strengthens the network — the "expelled node" carries trust capital to a new jurisdiction and opens a new connection.

6. The Somali Xawilaad — A Country Run on Remittances

Origin: Somalia, formalized post-1991 (collapse of central government)

Scale: $1.5-2 billion/year — roughly 25-40% of Somalia's GDP

How it works: After the Somali state collapsed, the diaspora (1-2 million people worldwide) became the country's de facto financial system. Xawilaad companies (like Dahabshiil, founded 1970) became shadow banks — handling deposits, loans, currency exchange, and even utility payments. A Somali in Minneapolis sends $200 through Dahabshiil; within hours, his mother in Mogadishu receives it at a local agent.

Why it's the most relevant case for network states:

  • A real-world example of a diaspora financially sustaining a nation without a functioning state
  • The system was nearly destroyed by post-9/11 AML regulations (Western banks cut off Somali remittance companies)
  • The diaspora community organized politically in the US and UK to protect the system — lobbying, advocacy, Oxfam partnerships
  • Some xawilaad companies are now transitioning into licensed banks

Design pattern for network states:

The diaspora IS the economy. Somalia proves that a scattered people can run a country's finances without a state — but also that you're vulnerable to other states' regulations. A network state needs regulatory strategy from day one. Crypto helps, but it's not immune to banking de-risking. Build multiple channels: stablecoins, licensed remittance corridors, AND trust-based informal systems as backup.

7. The Knights Templar — A Network State That Invented Banking

Origin: Jerusalem / Europe, 1119-1312 CE

Structure: International religious-military order with ~870 castles, estates, and commanderies across Europe and the Holy Land

How it works: A pilgrim deposits funds at the Temple Church in London and receives a letter of credit. He travels to Jerusalem — dangerous, months-long journey — carrying only the letter. At the Templar commandery in Jerusalem, he withdraws his money. The Templars were, literally, "the Western Union of the Crusades."

Why it matters:

  • The Templars were the first organization to operate a cross-border financial network independent of any single state
  • They were a network (distributed nodes across jurisdictions), a state (had diplomatic recognition, their own legal code, answered only to the Pope), and a bank — all at once
  • They were destroyed by the French king Philip IV in 1307, who owed them too much money — proving that network states that become creditors to nation-states become targets

Design pattern for network states:

Don't become too useful to a single sovereign. The Templars' destruction is the canonical cautionary tale. They had everything — distributed infrastructure, trusted brand, cross-border finance — but concentrated political risk in France. A network state should be useful to many jurisdictions and essential to none.

Synthesis: The Six Laws of Stateless Money

Across all seven systems, a set of recurring principles emerges:

Law 1: Trust Networks Beat Trust-Less Networks (For In-Group Transactions)

Hawala, susu, Armenian networks, Jewish bills — all built on high-trust communities, not trustless protocols. Crypto's "don't trust, verify" ethos works for interactions between strangers. But within a network state's citizenry, reputation-based systems are faster, cheaper, and build stronger bonds.

Law 2: The Unit of Account Follows the Community, Not the State

Every diaspora invents its own accounting: the hawaladar's ledger, the susu circle's rotation, the Julfan Armenian's commenda contract. Network states should think less about "what token do we issue" and more about "what value do we track among our people."

Law 3: Women Are the Core Infrastructure

Susu circles, family-based hawala networks, the household economies that sustain diaspora communities — women are disproportionately the operators. Network states with a tech-bro-only vibe are ignoring the demographic that has historically built and maintained community financial systems.

Law 4: Successful Parallel Systems Get Co-Opted or Destroyed

Fei-ch'ien → became state paper money. Jewish banking → became state-licensed banks. Xawilaad → under pressure to become licensed. Templars → destroyed. The lifecycle is: innovation → adoption → state attention → absorption or destruction. Plan for Phase 3.

Law 5: Portability Is Survival

Every system here was designed to move value without moving stuff — because stateless peoples can't rely on physical infrastructure. Network states should obsessively optimise for portability: of value, identity, reputation, and records. If your citizens can't carry everything in a phone and a passphrase, you're not portable enough.

Law 6: Financial Entanglement Creates Citizenship

The deepest insight: in all these systems, participating in the money network is what makes you a member. Not a passport, not a vote, not a flag. You're Somali because your family sends money through xawilaad. You're a Julfan Armenian because you honour the commenda. Monetary participation as citizenship is not a Web3 invention — it's the oldest form of national belonging for peoples without states.

What This Means for Belarus Network State 2030

Belarus has every ingredient:

  1. A motivated diaspora — 100K-500K Belarusians abroad since 2020, politically activated, digitally literate
  2. Existing trust infrastructure — Solidarity Fund, BYPOL, People's Embassies, mutual aid networks
  3. Cultural cohesion — shared language, recent shared trauma (2020 protests), strong identity markers
  4. Web3 literacy — Belarus had a disproportionately large crypto community pre-2020

What's missing is the explicit framing of financial solidarity as the first layer of statehood. Not "we'll issue a token someday" — but "participating in our mutual aid system is your first act of citizenship."

A Concrete Proposal: The Сяброўская Каса (Friendship Fund)

Inspired by susu + hawala + modern crypto rails:

  • Structure: Small circles of 10-15 diaspora Belarusians. Monthly contribution (€50-200). Rotating payout + emergency fund.
  • Tech layer: On-chain commitment contracts (Ethereum L2 or similar), but with social/reputation enforcement — exactly like traditional systems.
  • Identity layer: Each participant has a non-transferable "Syabr" (friend) credential. Participation history builds reputation.
  • Scale path: Circles → clusters → a network of clusters with inter-circle clearing (like hawala broker networks).
  • Political layer: The fund simultaneously tracks "GDP" — the total value circulating in the network. This becomes a dashboard of national economic activity, just like Somali remittance data proves diaspora economic power.

The point isn't the technology. The point is that 700 years of history show the same thing: a people who move money together become a nation.
