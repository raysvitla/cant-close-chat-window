I Can't Close
The Chat Window when tools start using you

I tracked my screen for sixteen days in late 2025. Standard productivity audit. The kind you do when you suspect your days are leaking somewhere but can't name the drain.

The audit found the drain. I hadn't written a single paragraph without consulting an AI in over three months.

Not because the model was required. I write for a living. I've written research reports that closed six-figure deals. I once wrote a 40-minute musical set and performed it on Kyryllivs'ka. The capacity was there. But somewhere between spring and autumn, I'd stopped trusting that I could produce adequate prose without the machine.

Ivan Illich had a word for this. In Medical Nemesis (1975), he called it iatrogenesis — when the healthcare system itself becomes the source of illness. Not malpractice. Something worse: the institution destroying the very capacity it claims to support. Patients who can no longer heal without hospitals, students who can no longer learn without schools.

I was looking at the cognitive version. Self-inflicted. Voluntary.

minsk → kyiv → lisbon

Some context on why this hit differently for me.

I've been displaced three times. Minsk to Kyiv to Lisbon. Each crossing stripped away another layer of institutional identity. Degrees that don't transfer. Professional networks that evaporate at the border. Reputation that means nothing in a new language. The Belarusian diploma that got me meetings in Minsk was decorative paper in Kyiv. The Kyiv network I rebuilt over two years became a contact list of people scattered across a dozen countries after February 2022.

What survives displacement is a raw decision-making capacity. You learn to operate without the scaffolding. No alma mater network. No legible career ladder. Just your judgment and whatever you can carry in your head across a border at short notice.

This is the thing about immigrants that people who've stayed put don't always see: we've already been stripped to the chassis. We know exactly what's ours and what was borrowed from the environment. Or at least we think we do.

The screen diary showed me that this capacity — the one thing displacement couldn't take — was being quietly mediated by a tool I'd invited in myself. Not imposed by a state. Not required by an employer. Chosen freely, daily, until the choosing became invisible.

I'd been through three borders. Each one taught me the same lesson: institutions will fail you, so build your own capacity. And then I handed that capacity to an API endpoint, one convenient autocomplete at a time.

illich's test

In Tools for Conviviality (1973), Illich proposed something like a test — though he'd probably hate me calling it that. He wasn't handing out evaluation frameworks. He was questioning the assumptions that make you think you need an evaluation framework at all. But I'm a consultant by training, so I read him and immediately started distilling criteria. Old habits.

Here's my distillation, not his checklist: A tool is convivial if anyone can use it without special certification. If it serves the user's purpose, not the creator's. If one person's use doesn't degrade it for another. And if it creates no obligation to keep using it.

When I first read these I thought — okay, this is obvious. Then I tried to name a single digital tool in my daily stack that passes all four. Couldn't.

the new radical monopoly

Take ChatGPT or Claude. The first three criteria arguably pass. Anyone with internet access can use them. They broadly serve user purposes. One person's query doesn't diminish another's.

The fourth criterion is where it collapses. Quietly, without anyone voting on it.

Job listings now require "AI proficiency." Universities permit AI-assisted essays — which sounds progressive until you realize unassisted essays now look suspiciously underperforming. Publishers assume AI editing as baseline. Clients ask why your research report doesn't have the polish of an AI-generated document.

Illich had a concept for this: radical monopoly. Cars didn't just outcompete buses. They rebuilt cities — highways, suburbs, parking lots — until walking stopped being a viable way to get around. The car didn't win a fair race. It redesigned the track.

I recognized the pattern from border crossings. At each one, I learned how deeply my capacity depended on institutional infrastructure I couldn't see until it vanished. Radical monopoly works the same way. You don't notice the dependency until you try to opt out and discover the world has been quietly restructured around the assumption that you won't.

AI is doing this to cognition. Not by being better than human thought, but by reshaping the environment until unaugmented thinking looks like walking on the freeway.

Running your own model on your own hardware gets closer to convivial — no subscription, no data leaving your machine. But a decent local setup requires a thousand-dollar GPU and command-line literacy that maybe five percent of the population has. The convivial tool that demands expertise recreates the professional monopoly Illich found in medicine: healing confiscated by doctors, patients needing permission to understand their own bodies. Self-hosting is convivial for the technically literate. For everyone else, it's a monastery with a very specific entrance exam.

Crypto has the same structure. Self-custodial wallets — your keys, your money, no bank, no intermediary. Beautiful architecture. Then you hit seed phrases, gas fees, bridge protocols, smart contract approvals you can't read. The complexity creates its own priesthood: a new mediating class between you and the tool that was supposed to eliminate mediating classes. The tool promising liberation from institutions generates its own institution.

the crossing moment

I can trace the specific inversion in my own workflow. I wasn't watching for it. I went looking after the screen diary made it impossible to ignore.

Early 2025: think → draft outline → use Claude for research → write the piece myself
Late 2025: open Claude → describe what I want to think about → generate draft → edit output
March 2026: tell Promen what to think about → Promen spawns critics, editors, sub-agents → approve

Same tools. Same person. The arrows point in opposite directions. In the first, I'm using a tool. In the second, I'm supervising one.

My credit lines shifted from "designed by Ray, drafted with Claude" to "drafted by Promen, approved by Ray." Two words swapped. The entire creative relationship inverted.

I noticed it the day I needed to write my own contract terms. Equity split, bonuses, KPIs — for the company I work for. The document that defines my own professional life. I know this stuff cold. I opened a chat with my AI assistant and started typing.

Not because I couldn't do it. Because it didn't occur to me to try. The blank page wasn't hostile — it was simply absent from the workflow. The step where I sit down and think through what I actually want, what I'm worth, what I refuse — that step had been replaced by "describe it to the model and let it draft something." For my own contract. The terms of my own labor, drafted by a machine I prompted.

I'm writing this essay the same way. Right now, March 2026. An essay about AI dependency, produced through an AI pipeline. I told Promen what I wanted to say. Promen spawned eight critics, ran three rounds of review, rewrote the second half twice. I approved.

Every individual decision made sense. I'm busy. The tool is fast. Why wouldn't I use it? But you zoom out over three months and the pattern is clear: I stopped writing. I started approving.

shadow work

I don't even write prompts anymore. I build Skills. Reusable instruction sets that tell my AI agents how to behave, what voice to use, which critics to spawn, how many rounds to run. I build them for myself and for others. I named my agents. Promen writes my essays. Stacy handles research. I'm not prompting a model. I'm architecting a workforce.

Last week I spent ninety minutes building a Skill for a research task I could have done myself in two hours. I felt productive the entire time. Then I looked at what I'd actually done. I'd designed someone else's job and called it creative practice.

Illich had a name for this kind of labor. In Shadow Work (1981), he described the invisible complement to wage labor — the unpaid work the industrial system requires to function. The housework that enables the wage earner. The commute that delivers you to the office. The self-checkout that hands you the cashier's job for free.

Skill architecture is shadow work. The hours designing agent behaviors, structuring workflows, testing critic pipelines, tuning voice parameters. This is labor that trains future models, generates value for the platform, keeps the ecosystem growing. And unlike the self-checkout, which at least feels like work, building Skills feels like craft. It feels like management. Nobody forces you to do it, and Illich's shadow work was compulsory, the commute you couldn't refuse. But that voluntariness makes it more extractive, not less. You don't recognize the labor as labor because you chose it.

what the tool took last

I keep thinking the story here is about writing. About cognition. About the slow erosion of a skill I used to trust. That's the version that's safe to publish. The screen diary, the blank page, the system prompt that ate ninety minutes — it's uncomfortable, but it's contained. It's about work.

The thing I haven't said yet isn't about work.

A few weeks ago, my wife was going through something difficult. I'll leave the details out — they're hers, not mine. What matters is what I did.

I didn't sit down with her. I didn't ask how she was feeling. I didn't do the thing that a person who loves another person does, which is to be present in the room with them and listen.

I opened a chat with my AI assistant. And I started typing out her situation. Describing it. Analyzing it. Breaking it into components. Looking for patterns. Asking the model what I should do.

She was in the same room

Illich wrote about the Samaritan — a man sees someone suffering and stops. No institution, no protocol. Just one person in the room with another. Illich's fear was that we'd systematize that response until the impulse to stop disappears. I didn't stop. I opened a chat window.

I don't know how to end this cleanly. I don't think I should.
